\subsection*{Anderson acceleration: convergence and diagnostics}
\label{subsec:anderson}
\paragraph{Motivation and scope}
To reduce the number of block fixed-point (block Picard/Gauss--Seidel) sub-iterations, we employ Type-II Anderson acceleration (AA-II) with finite memory $m$. We compare (i) a plain block Picard baseline and (ii) AA-II with standard safeguards (restart on deterioration and backtracking/damping) across representative couplings and time-step sizes $\Delta t$. We also report diagnostics that help choose~$m$ and monitor robustness.

\subsection*{Interaction-gain diagnostics and spectral radius}\label{subsec:diag_rhoJ}
We estimate a $3\times 3$ interaction-gain matrix $J$ of the Gauss--Seidel (GS) fixed-point map $G$ at the current state by finite-difference GS sweeps on the accelerated remodeling state $(\rho,\mathbf L,S)$: for each block we form a small deterministic perturbation $\delta_j$ and measure
\begin{equation*}
  J_{ij}\;\approx\;\frac{\big\|\,G_i(x^\star+\delta_j)-G_i(x^\star)\,\big\|}{\|\delta_j\|}\,.
\end{equation*}
The block norms $\|\cdot\|$ are the same as in our stopping criterion (\S\ref{subsec:proj_merit}); for mechanics we \emph{project out} Dirichlet degrees of freedom before forming the norm. We report the spectral radius $\rho(J)$ as a diagnostic of local contractivity ($\rho(J)<1$ implies a locally contracting GS map in this block-norm sense). This diagnostic is optional and does not affect the iterate; it can be enabled per sub-iteration.

\paragraph{Contractivity analysis}
We summarize contractivity and coupling structure across timesteps and configurations (\cref{fig:contractivity}). Panel (a) shows the evolution of the spectral radius $\rho(J)$ over time with the $\rho(J)=1$ threshold; values below unity indicate local contraction. Panel (b) visualizes a representative interaction-gain matrix $\mathbf J$ as a heatmap; panel (c) tracks the strongest gains over time. Panel (d) aggregates the distribution of $\rho(J)$ across $\Delta t$, and panel (e) shows the maximum off-diagonal gain versus time. Together these diagnostics indicate predominantly contractive behavior and stable coupling strengths in the regimes tested.

\subsection*{Projected residual merit, stopping, and AA acceptance}\label{subsec:proj_merit}
We use the \emph{projected, RMS-per-DOF} merit and acceptance/backtracking defined in the solver description (\S\ref{sec:fp-aa}). The projected norm acts on the flattened state $(\rho,\mathbf L,S)$, zeros any constrained DOFs, and uses block weights $w_i=1/N_i$; acceptance follows the safeguard \eqref{eq:safeguard} with the same thresholds and minimal-subiteration requirement as in \S\ref{sec:fp-aa}. Candidate formation and step limiting follow \eqref{eq:kkt}--\eqref{eq:aa-update}.

\subsection*{Stacking vs.\ sweep order}\label{subsec:order}
The accelerated stacked state follows $(\rho,\mathbf L,S)$, while a single GS sweep updates blocks in the physical order $u\!\to\! S\!\to\!\rho\!\to\!\mathbf L$ (mechanics is recomputed inside the driver and is not part of the Anderson state). The interaction-gain matrix $J$ is indexed according to the $(\rho,\mathbf L,S)$ ordering.

\subsection*{Anderson acceleration details}\label{subsec:aa_details}
We employ the Type-II Anderson (Pulay) acceleration scheme described in \S\ref{sec:fp-aa}.
Specific settings for the results below include a step limit factor $c=2$ and history depths $m$ as indicated in the figures.
We restart the AA history upon (i) repeated rejections, (ii) stagnation with respect to the best Picard merit, or (iii) poor conditioning of the regularized Gram system. Accepted AA candidates re-enforce Dirichlet DOFs from the freshest Picard state.

\paragraph{Performance diagnostics}
To assess stability and tuning, we monitor Anderson metrics over time and their sensitivity to $(\Delta t, m, \beta)$ (\cref{fig:aa-metrics}). At fixed $\Delta t=25$ days, a sliding-window view of the Gram-matrix condition number $\kappa(\mathbf H)$ remains well behaved across tested histories $m$ and mixing factors $\beta$. Aggregated over late-time windows, the rejection rate stays moderate, the average history depth scales with $m$, and the median $\kappa(\mathbf H)$ shows only mild growth with larger $m$ or $\beta$.

\paragraph{AA vs. Picard}
We compare the baseline block Picard scheme with AA-II across representative timestep sizes and coupling strengths (\cref{fig:aa-vs-picard}). In our runs, AA-II consistently reduces sub-iterations per timestep and total walltime while preserving acceptance and stability safeguards; the benefit is largest in tighter couplings, with comparable behavior to Picard in mild regimes.
