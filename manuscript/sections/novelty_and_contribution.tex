\section*{Contribution and Novelty}
\label{sec:contribution-novelty}
\paragraph{Contribution and Novelty.}
We introduce a smoothly regularized \emph{three-field} continuum formulation of bone remodeling in which the state is given by the apparent density, the logarithmic fabric, and a nonlocal mechanotransductive stimulus, while the displacement is obtained at each iteration from quasi-static mechanical equilibrium and is not treated as an evolving state variable. The methodological novelty is fourfold: (i) anisotropy is advanced directly in log-fabric space and coupled to elasticity through a fabric-guided orthotropic law, with eigenvalue-ratio bounds and consistently smoothed clamping that enforce physical admissibility without hard projections; (ii) all spectral quantities required by constitutive evaluation and by the construction of the trace-free logarithmic fabric target are computed using \emph{robust Sylvester spectral projectors} evaluated without eigenvectors and smoothly blended to the isotropic and transversely isotropic limits, with explicit enforcement of the partition of unity to prevent basis flips and discontinuities near eigenvalue degeneracy; (iii) mechanotransduction is promoted from a local index to an explicit stimulus field governed by diffusion--reaction dynamics and driven by cycle-weighted mechanical cues, and density evolves via bounded formation/resorption kinetics augmented by spatial regularization to suppress checkerboarding and mesh artifacts; and (iv) the fully implicit, tightly coupled update is advanced by a modular partitioned block Gauss--Seidel strategy (mechanics $\rightarrow$ fabric $\rightarrow$ stimulus $\rightarrow$ density) stabilized by Pulay/DIIS (type-II Anderson) mixing with step limiting and adaptive history management, yielding stable, quasi mesh-independent convergence without forming monolithic Jacobians. Beyond the formulation, we provide a reproducible MPI-parallel reference implementation (FEniCSx/PETSc) with scalable parallel I/O and automated verification, and we demonstrate the approach on controlled benchmarks and a CT-derived proximal femur case study, including sensitivity analyses that quantify the impact of plausible loading variations on predicted density redistribution.
