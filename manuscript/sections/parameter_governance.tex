\section*{Parameter governance}
\label{sec:parameter-governance}
\paragraph{Units and scaling.}
We use the unit system: length [mm], time [day], stress/energy [MPa], and density [g/cm$^3$] (see \S\ref{sec:strong-forms-nd}). The stimulus $S$ is dimensionless, the apparent density $\rho$ is in g/cm$^3$, and fabric quantities ($\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{L}$) are dimensionless.

\paragraph{Mechanical parameters.}
Apparent densities in human cancellous bone span roughly $0.05$--$1.1~\mathrm{g/cm^3}$, while cortical bone is commonly reported around $1.7$--$2.0~\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ \cite{Helgason2008,Rho1993}. This motivates bounds $\rho_{\min}$ (numerical floor) and $\rho_{\max}$; in practice $\rho_{\min}$ is primarily a conditioning parameter for the mechanics. For the isotropic baseline, $\nu_0\approx 0.3$ is typical and cortical Young's moduli are on the order of $15$--$25~\mathrm{GPa}$ \cite{Rho1993}. For the density--stiffness scaling in \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}, adaptive remodeling studies commonly use power-law exponents in the range $n_{\mathrm{trab}}\approx 2$--$3$ for trabecular bone and $n_{\mathrm{cort}}\approx 1$--$2$ for denser tissue \cite{Weinans1992_BehaviorAdaptiveBoneRemodeling,Keller1994_PredictingCompressiveMechanicalBehavior,Helgason2008}. The transition densities $\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max}$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}$ should bracket the trabecular--cortical overlap so that the blended exponent $k(\widetilde{\rho})$ varies only over a physiologically plausible interval. Finally, the fabric exponents $p_E$ and $p_G$ primarily control how strongly anisotropy is expressed in stiffness; values near $1$ are typical in fabric-based orthotropic scaling \cite{Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}, and $p_E,p_G\in[0.5,2]$ is a conservative range unless a dedicated calibration dataset is available.

\paragraph{Stimulus parameters.}
Peak physiological strains in load-bearing bone are on the order of $10^{-3}$ to a few $10^{-3}$ (hundreds to a few thousand microstrain) \cite{Turner1998_ThreeRulesBone,Robling2006}. For apparent moduli in the $1$--$20~\mathrm{GPa}$ range this implies strain-energy densities on the order of $10^{-3}$--$10^{-1}~\mathrm{MPa}$ (via $\psi\sim\tfrac12 E\varepsilon^2$), so the reference specific SED values $\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{trab}}$ and $\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{cort}}$ should be chosen in that ballpark once the loading magnitude and stiffness scaling are fixed. Weinans et al.\ used a reference value $\hat{\psi}_{\mathrm{ref}}=0.25~\mathrm{J/g}$ for energy per mass \cite{Weinans1992_BehaviorAdaptiveBoneRemodeling}, highlighting that these references are ultimately calibration parameters tied to the applied loads. The averaging exponent $p$ in \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd} controls how strongly $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ is peak-biased; $p\in[1,6]$ spans the range from a weighted mean ($p=1$) to a strongly peak-dominated driver. The lazy-zone half-width $\delta_0$ controls the tolerated deviation around the set-point before net formation/resorption; practical values are typically $\delta_0\approx 0.05$--$0.3$. The saturation parameters $(S_{\max},\kappa_S)$ should bound $S$ without clipping physiologically relevant values; $S_{\max}\sim 0.5$--$2$ with $\kappa_S\sim 0.1$--$1$ are robust defaults. For the transport parameters, $\tau_S$ should be short relative to density remodeling (days to a few weeks), and $D_S$ can be chosen to produce a sensing length $\ell_S=\sqrt{\tau_S D_S}$ on the order of $10^{-1}$--$10^{0}~\mathrm{mm}$, depending on the intended degree of nonlocal averaging.

\paragraph{Density parameters.}
Weinans et al.\ reported $\rho_{\max}\approx 1.74~\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ and a near-resorption floor $\rho_{\min}\approx 0.01~\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ in simulation studies \cite{Weinans1992_BehaviorAdaptiveBoneRemodeling}. In practice, larger numerical floors (e.g.\ $\rho_{\min}\gtrsim 0.05$) improve conditioning if very low-density regions cause mechanical ill-conditioning, while keeping $\rho_{\min}$ small is appropriate if complete resorption is part of the intended biology. The kinetic gains $(k_{\mathrm{form}},k_{\mathrm{res}})$ set the remodeling time scale; to represent adult adaptation on the order of months, implied characteristic rates are typically $O(10^{-3}$--$10^{-2})~\mathrm{d^{-1}}$ when $S=O(1)$ \cite{Robling2006}. The diffusion coefficient $D_\rho$ primarily acts as a regularization length scale; choosing $D_\rho$ so that $\ell_\rho=\sqrt{T D_\rho}$ is on the order of $0.5$--$2~\mathrm{mm}$ over a remodeling horizon $T$ (tens to hundreds of days) is generally sufficient to suppress mesh-scale oscillations without over-smoothing. If the surface-availability factor $\alpha_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho)$ is enabled (cf.\ \eqref{eq:Asurf-nd}), $\alpha_{\min}$ and $a_{v,0}$ should prevent kinetics from freezing at high density while still reflecting reduced available surface in dense tissue \cite{Pivonka2013_InfluenceBoneSurfaceAvailability}.

\paragraph{Fabric parameters.}
Microstructural reorientation is expected to evolve on the remodeling time scale (months) rather than on the loading time scale; consequently $\tau_A\sim 30$--$300~\mathrm{d}$ is a reasonable prior if fabric represents remodeling-driven alignment \cite{Robling2006}. The diffusion coefficient $D_A$ sets a smoothing length $\sqrt{T D_A}$ and can be tuned to the targeted spatial correlation length of anisotropy. The exponent $\gamma_F$ controls how strongly stress-eigenvalue contrasts translate into fabric-eigenvalue ratios; $\gamma_F\in[0.5,2]$ is conservative (with $\gamma_F=1$ a neutral default). The bounds $m_{\min},m_{\max}$ cap admissible anisotropy; the ratio $m_{\max}/m_{\min}$ limits the maximum eigenvalue ratio and should be chosen to avoid numerically extreme stiffness contrasts. Finally, the regularization parameters $\varepsilon_Q$ and $\varepsilon_a$ should be small relative to the scale of $\mathbf{Q}$ so that they only stabilize eigen-decomposition and activity gating near isotropy.

\subsection{Parameter Sets for Literature-Based Validation and Numerical Benchmarking}

To rigorously assess both the physiological plausibility and the numerical robustness of the proposed framework, we define two distinct parameter configurations: the \textit{PhysioSetup} and the \textit{StiffSetup} (summarized in Table~\ref{tab:param_setup}).

The \textbf{PhysioSetup} represents a baseline physiological state, calibrated according to established values reported in the literature. It employs kinetic rates and regularization length scales ($D_\rho, D_S$) consistent with prior computational studies, ensuring smooth morphological transitions that reflect the typical adaptation time scales of human bone.

In contrast, the \textbf{StiffSetup} is designed as a synthetic numerical ``stress test'' to evaluate solver stability under extreme conditions. By significantly narrowing the mechanostat's lazy zone ($\delta_0$) and saturation width ($\kappa_S$), and by accelerating formation/resorption gains ($k_{form}, k_{res}$) by an order of magnitude, we induce a highly stiff, reaction-dominated regime. This configuration generates sharp internal gradients and rapid temporal transients that challenge the convergence of standard fixed-point iterations, thereby serving as a rigorous benchmark for the proposed Anderson-accelerated solver.

\paragraph{Time step size.}
A conservative time-step choice follows the fastest intrinsic time scales: in \textbf{PhysioSetup} the stimulus and fabric relax over $\tau_S \approx 25\,\mathrm{d}$ and $\tau_A \approx 50\,\mathrm{d}$, so resolving transients suggests $\Delta t \sim 0.05\!-\!0.2\,\tau_S \approx 1\!-\!5\,\mathrm{d}$; density kinetics are typically slower and therefore less restrictive. In \textbf{StiffSetup}, accelerated formation/resorption and a sharper mechanostat imply that density kinetics dominate, so a conservative rule is $\Delta t \sim 0.05\!-\!0.2\min(\rho_{\max}/k_{\mathrm{form}},\,\rho_{\min}/k_{\mathrm{res}})$, often yielding sub-day steps (e.g., $\sim 0.05\!-\!0.2\,\mathrm{d}$). Expected settling to quasi-steady behavior occurs over a few time constants: stimulus $\sim 3\tau_S$ (tens of days), fabric $\sim 3\tau_A$ (order $10^2$ days), and density over a few multiples of its dominant kinetic scale (months in Physio, potentially weeks in Stiff). Note that numerical convergence can still hold for much larger $\Delta t$ because the stimulus is bounded and saturating ($\tanh$, $|S|\le S_{\max}$) and density rates self-limit near $\rho_{\min},\rho_{\max}$; such large steps should be viewed as steady-state accelerators unless verified by step-refinement ($\Delta t$ vs.\ two $\Delta t/2$).
