\begin{abstract}
We propose a four-field bone-remodeling formulation that co-evolves displacement $\mathbf{u}$, apparent density $\rho$, log-fabric $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$, and a nonlocal mechanotransductive stimulus $S$. The elastic response follows a fabric-guided orthotropic law, where stiffness tensors are constructed from the spectral decomposition of $\mathbf{L}$. A cycle-weighted strain-energy driver is filtered by a diffusion--reaction stimulus with a smooth saturating (lazy-zone) mechanostat; density evolves via bounded formation/resorption kinetics with diffusive regularization; and log-fabric relaxes toward a trace-free logarithmic target constructed from cycle-weighted \emph{deviatoric} stress outer-products, with spatial diffusion and activity gating. Time integration is fully implicit Backward--Euler; space is discretized by conforming $H^1$ finite elements in a unified weak-form notation. Nonlinear coupling is solved by a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel sweep (mechanics $\rightarrow$ fabric $\rightarrow$ stimulus $\rightarrow$ density) wrapped in Type-II Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and residual safeguards. A single smoothing policy used across clamping and normalizations ensures differentiability and preserves coercivity, while the solver architecture yields stable, mesh-robust convergence without monolithic Jacobians.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
\label{s:introduction}
Bone is a living composite that continually reorganizes its microstructure to balance mechanical function with metabolic cost. Four mechanobiologically distinct processes drive this adaptation: (i) the elastic response of the solid matrix to loads, (ii) the evolution of mass (apparent density/porosity), (iii) the reorientation of the trabecular/collagen network (fabric anisotropy), and (iv) the production and spatial transport of cell-mediated biochemical signals that couple local mechanics to osteoblast/osteoclast activity. Robust numerical prediction of morphology and stiffness therefore requires a model that co-evolves these \emph{four} fields---displacement $u$, density $\rho$, fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$, and a diffusive mechanotransductive stimulus $S$---within a single, tightly coupled formulation.

Mechanobiology supports this coupling. First, the mechanical field is not merely a passive input: Turner’s “three rules” assert that the magnitude, rate (frequency), and rest intervals of \emph{dynamic} loading govern the osteogenic response \cite{Turner1998_ThreeRulesBone}. Spatial \emph{strain gradients}---proxies for lacuno--canalicular (LCN) fluid flow---localize bone formation more reliably than strain magnitude alone, as shown in turkey radius models \cite{Gross1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate} and in exercise-induced periosteal activation in rooster models \cite{Zernicke1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate}. These gradients act through osteocytes embedded in the LCN, where fluid shear induces Ca$^{2+}$/Wnt signaling; acute loading suppresses sclerostin (Sost), while unloading elevates it, shifting the local formation/resorption balance \cite{Robling2008_MechanicalStimulationBone}. While mechanosensitive channels such as PIEZO1 contribute to skeletal homeostasis, their cell-type-specific roles in load adaptation are nuanced and do not obviate the need to resolve the full biophysical cascade from matrix strains to fluid shear to osteocytic signaling \cite{Wang2020_MechanicalSensingProtein}. Together, these findings motivate an explicit stimulus field $S$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical cues and \emph{transported} over osteocytic/vascular pathways (e.g., via diffusion--decay dynamics), rather than a purely local, algebraic “mechanical index.”

Second, accurate mechanics--morphology feedback requires density $\rho$ and anisotropy $\mathbf{A}$ to co-evolve and feed back into the elastic moduli. Empirically, bone volume fraction (BV/TV) explains most variance in cancellous stiffness, and fabric anisotropy accounts for nearly all of the remainder; together they reach $\sim$95--98\% explanatory power across anatomical sites, with negligible contribution from other morphometric covariates \cite{Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Theoretical and constitutive links between $\{\rho,\mathbf{A}\}$ and orthotropic elastic constants are well established---Cowin’s fabric--elasticity relations (1985, 1990) and the Zysset--Curnier orthotropic model (1995) connect BV/TV and second-order fabric tensors to the apparent stiffness tensor (including $E$, $G$, and $\nu$) \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}. In practice, fabric can be quantified directly from imaging using gray-level structure-tensor or mean-intercept-length methods and validated on HR-pQCT/$\mu$CT, enabling evolution laws for $\mathbf{A}$ aligned with principal stress/flow directions \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Ali2017_Mu, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}. As $\rho$ and $\mathbf{A}$ change through remodeling, the spatial stiffness field---and thus the displacement field $u$ and its gradients---must be updated consistently to avoid spurious transients and mesh-dependent artifacts; morphology--elasticity models with full fabric information achieve the highest predictive accuracy in such coupled simulations \cite{Schneider2012_MorphologyElasticityRelationships, Panyasantis2016_EffectiveElasticProperties}.

Third, the remodeling stimulus that drives BMU-scale biology is not localized to a voxel or finite element. Experiments and theory both indicate spatial nonlocality through osteocytic/vascular networks; modern formulations therefore promote the stimulus to a \emph{field} obeying a diffusion--reaction equation, which systematically removes unphysical checkerboarding and captures healing or damage--remodeling interactions \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. Recent work also explores multi-stimulus energetics (e.g., shear vs.\ normal components) in continuum settings, reinforcing the need to track multiple evolving material parameters beyond a single density variable \cite{Branecka2024_BoneRemodelingModel}.

From a numerical standpoint, loosely coupled or explicit updates are in general more fragile than monolithic schemes: density-only algorithms are prone to mesh-dependent checkerboards and near-field oscillations near load singularities \cite{Jacobs1995_NumericalInstabilitiesBone, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. We therefore retain a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel fixed point and wrap it in Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and safeguards; see the solver description in \S\ref{sec:fp-aa}. This combination substantially mitigates splitting artifacts and yields robust, quasi mesh-independent convergence in our experiments \cite{Both2019_AndersonAcceleratedFixed, Walker2011_AndersonAcceleration, Fang2009_TwoClassesMultisecant} while remaining minimally invasive on top of standard FE components.

\section*{Comparison with related models and novelty}
\label{sec:related-novelty}
Our formulation sits between classical density--only mechanostat models and fully coupled cell--population or damage--remodeling frameworks. In \emph{local} mechanostat models the remodeling drive is a pointwise function of a mechanical index, typically strain or strain--energy density, and only the density $\rho$ evolves; transport and fabric are absent and spatial regularity is supplied ad hoc (cf.\ early density models and follow--ups reviewed in \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological}). Nonlocal and \emph{diffusive} stimulus fields---as in \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd}---have been advocated to account for osteocytic/vascular communication and to remove checkerboarding; this strand has gained traction in the last decade \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach}. On the elasticity side, orthotropic models that couple BV/TV and second--order fabric tensors to the full fourth--order stiffness tensor (Cowin's relations and the Zysset--Curnier model) are now standard in homogenized micro--FE and hFE pipelines \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic, Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Our constitutive choice preserves this spirit but adopts an energetically nonnegative, rank--one reinforcement aligned with a unit--trace direction proxy $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$, which empirically captures the dominant directional effect while simplifying the numerics; it leaves the isotropic floor strictly coercive through the smooth clamp $\widetilde\rho\ge\rho_{\min}$, cf.\ \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}.

In the \emph{evolution} of anisotropy, several authors align fabric with stress or strain measures and regularize it by diffusion or smoothing (e.g.\ structure--tensor approaches and stress--alignment hypotheses, see \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}). We follow this line by evolving the log-fabric $\mathbf{L}=\ln\mathbf{A}$ with a relaxation--diffusion flow toward a trace-free logarithmic target constructed from the cycle-averaged \emph{deviatoric} stress outer-product,
\(
\bar{\mathbf{Q}}=\mathrm{sym}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{dev}}\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{dev}}^{\!\top})
\)
with $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{dev}}:=\boldsymbol{\sigma}-\tfrac13(\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\sigma})\mathbf{I}$, using smooth regularization and eigenvalue-ratio bounds to avoid non-differentiable spectral projections and extreme anisotropy.

\section*{Governing equations}
\label{sec:strong-forms-nd}
\paragraph{Units and scaling.}
We use the unit system: length [mm], time [day], stress/energy [MPa], and density [g/cm$^3$]. The stimulus $S$ is dimensionless, the apparent density $\rho$ is in g/cm$^3$, and fabric quantities ($\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{L}$) are dimensionless. The boundary partition is $\partial\Omega=\Gamma_D\cup\Gamma_N$ with outward unit normal $\mathbf{n}$.

\subsection*{Mechanical submodel}
\label{subsec:mech-nd}
\paragraph{Kinematics and equilibrium.}
We adopt small-strain kinematics with the symmetric gradient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-eps-nd}
\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}) := \tfrac12\big(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^\top\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}_{\rm sym},\qquad d\in\{2,3\}.
\end{equation}
Under quasi-static conditions the balance of linear momentum reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-strong-nd}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_N.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Constitutive structure.}
We adopt the Zysset-Curnier orthotropic material model \cite{Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}, which defines the stiffness tensor $\mathbb{C}$ based on the apparent density $\rho$ and the fabric eigenvalue ratios $m_1, m_2, m_3$ obtained from the log-fabric field $\mathbf{L}$. The fabric tensor is recovered from $\mathbf{L}$ via the matrix exponential $\mathbf{A} = \exp(\mathbf{L})$.
The orthotropic engineering constants are given by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:zysset-curnier}
\begin{aligned}
    E_i &= E_0 \left(\frac{\widetilde{\rho}}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^{k(\rho)} m_i^{p_E}, \\
    G_{ij} &= \frac{E_0}{2(1+\nu_0)} \left(\frac{\widetilde{\rho}}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^{k(\rho)} (m_i m_j)^{p_G/2}, \\
    \nu_{ij} &= \nu_0 \quad \text{for } i<j, \\
    \nu_{ji} &= \nu_{ij}\,\frac{E_j}{E_i} \quad \text{for } i<j,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Orthotropic tensor construction and physical admissibility.}
The coefficients \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier} define engineering constants in the \emph{principal fabric frame}.
Let $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ denote the \emph{deviatoric (unit-determinant) normalization} of the fabric tensor, defined by \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}. We denote by $\{\mathbf{a}_i\}_{i=1}^3$ the orthonormal eigenvectors of $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ and by $\{m_i\}_{i=1}^3$ its eigenvalues, which represent \emph{eigenvalue ratios} (anisotropy factors) with isotropic scaling removed.
The stiffness operator entering $\boldsymbol{\sigma}=\mathbb{C}:\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is conveniently expressed in projector form using the spectral decomposition
\(
\widehat{\mathbf{A}}=\sum_{i=1}^3 m_i\,\mathbf{P}_i
\)
with $\mathbf{P}_i:=\mathbf{a}_i\otimes\mathbf{a}_i$ in the fully anisotropic case. Conceptually, one may assemble $\mathbb{C}$ by forming the orthotropic compliance matrix $\mathbf{S}$ from $\{E_i,G_{ij},\nu_{ij}\}$ (Voigt notation), enforcing reciprocity ($\nu_{ji}E_i=\nu_{ij}E_j$), inverting $\mathbf{S}$, and rotating to the spatial frame.
This construction ensures that the resulting constitutive operator is \emph{conservative} (minor and major symmetries) and admits a standard strain energy density $U=\tfrac12\boldsymbol{\sigma}:\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$.
In addition to the lower-density clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} (which guarantees strictly positive moduli in \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}), we ensure \emph{strong ellipticity} through admissible parameter ranges and bounded anisotropy (via eigenvalue-ratio bounds), and the law reduces smoothly to the isotropic baseline as $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}\to\mathbf{I}$.

where $E_0$ is a reference Young's modulus, $\nu_0$ is a (density-independent) Poisson ratio, $\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is a reference density, and $p_E$ and $p_G$ are stiffness exponents controlling axial and shear scaling with fabric. The density-dependent exponent $k(\rho)$ transitions smoothly from a trabecular exponent $n_{\mathrm{trab}}$ to a cortical exponent $n_{\mathrm{cort}}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variable-exponent}
k(\rho) = n_{\mathrm{trab}} (1 - w(\rho)) + n_{\mathrm{cort}} w(\rho),
\end{equation}
where $w(\rho)$ is a smooth interpolation function (smoothstep) transitioning from 0 to 1 over the range $[\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max}, \rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}]$. The density $\widetilde{\rho}$ is smoothly clamped to a minimum value $\rho_{\min}$ to ensure positive definiteness. The stress is then $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathbb{C} : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$.

\paragraph{Numerically robust stress evaluation.}
Two numerical issues arise in practice: (i) the projector/eigenvector representation is ill-conditioned when $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ is (nearly) isotropic or transversely isotropic (repeated eigenvalues), and (ii) extreme anisotropy can cause overflow in $\exp(\mathbf{L})$ and large stiffness contrasts. We address (i) by evaluating spectral projectors $\mathbf{P}_i$ without eigenvectors using the Sylvester formula and smoothly blending it to the isotropic limit ($\mathbf{P}_i=\mathbf{I}/3$) and the transversely isotropic limit (unique projector plus an equal split of the orthogonal plane), which avoids basis flips and preserves $\sum_i \mathbf{P}_i=\mathbf{I}$. We address (ii) by clamping the deviatoric log-eigenvalues of $\mathbf{L}$ before exponentiation, consistently with the global anisotropy bounds $m_{\min},m_{\max}$, and by using a smooth, scale-aware gate that blends the orthotropic stress to the isotropic baseline as $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}\to\mathbf{I}$, keeping $\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{L})$ continuous through eigenvalue degeneracy. Figure \ref{fig:sylvester_projectors} summarizes these limits and the associated conditioning behavior.

\paragraph{Smooth lower density clamp.}
To maintain uniform strong ellipticity and avoid case distinctions in highly porous regions, we use a smooth ``max'' operator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smooth-max-nd}
\widetilde{\rho}(\rho;\rho_{\min},\varepsilon)
:=\rho_{\min}+\tfrac12\!\left[(\rho-\rho_{\min})+\sqrt{(\rho-\rho_{\min})^2+\varepsilon^2}-\varepsilon\right].
\end{equation}
This monotone, everywhere differentiable approximation of $\max\{\rho,\rho_{\min}\}$ enforces $\widetilde{\rho}\ge\rho_{\min}$ with a single global smoothing width $\varepsilon>0$. No upper clamp is imposed.

\paragraph{Log-Euclidean Fabric Framework.}
To ensure the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$ remains symmetric and positive-definite (SPD) throughout the simulation, we evolve its matrix logarithm $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$. The fabric tensor is recovered via $\mathbf{A} = \exp(\mathbf{L})$. This parameterization guarantees that $\mathbf{A}$ is always SPD for any real symmetric tensor $\mathbf{L}$, avoiding the need for ad-hoc projections or constraints.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Figure \ref{fig:constitutive_law} illustrates the behavior of the density-elasticity law. Panel (a) shows the stiffness $E(\rho)$ transitioning from a quadratic trabecular regime ($n_{\mathrm{trab}}=2.0$) to a near-linear cortical regime ($n_{\mathrm{cort}}=1.3$). This dual-power law captures the stiffening efficiency of dense bone while maintaining the rapid compliance increase in porous bone. Panel (b) explicitly plots the exponent $n(\rho)$, which varies smoothly between the two regimes via the cubic smoothstep function shown in (f). Panels (c) and (d) demonstrate the sensitivity of the stiffness curve to the transition thresholds $\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max}$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}$; shifting these parameters allows for calibration to specific anatomical sites where the trabecular-cortical boundary may differ. Panel (e) highlights the effect of the exponents themselves; higher $n_{\mathrm{trab}}$ suppresses stiffness in low-density regions, effectively modeling the loss of connectivity in osteoporotic bone. The smooth lower clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} guarantees strictly positive stiffness even in highly porous regions, thus preventing mechanical degeneracy and preserving differentiability. The normalization \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} removes isotropic scaling from the fabric and yields a symmetric, unit-determinant anisotropy descriptor ($\det\widehat{\mathbf{A}}=1$), which is positive definite by construction. This combination yields a mechanically transparent, numerically robust model suitable for coupling with remodeling stimuli and evolution equations.

\subsection*{Stimulus submodel}\label{subsec:stim-nd}
We represent the mechanotransductive drive by a scalar field $S:\Omega\times(0,T]\to\mathbb{R}$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical activity and \emph{transported} through osteocytic/vascular pathways. Positive values correspond to net formation drive and negative values to net resorption drive.

The stimulus obeys a diffusion--reaction balance with a smooth saturating (lazy-zone) mechanostat drive computed from a daily mechanical stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-strong-nd}
\tau_S\,\partial_t S \;-\; \tau_S D_S\,\Delta S \;+\; S
\;=\; S_{\max}\,\tanh\!\Big(\delta_{\mathrm{eff}}(\psi_{\mathrm{day}},\rho)/\kappa_S\Big)
\quad\text{in }\Omega\times(0,T],
\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous Neumann boundary conditions and an initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}
\nabla S\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\ \ \text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
S(\cdot,0)=S_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $\tau_S\ge 0$ is the stimulus time constant, $D_S\ge 0$ the stimulus diffusivity, $S_{\max}>0$ a cap on $|S|$, and $\kappa_S>0$ controls the saturation width in the $\tanh$ response. The drive is built from a normalized deviation in density-normalized strain energy,
\[
m(x):=\frac{\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x)}{\widetilde\rho(x)},\qquad
m_{\mathrm{ref}}:=\frac{\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}},\qquad
\delta:=\frac{m-m_{\mathrm{ref}}}{m_{\mathrm{ref}}}\,,
\]
where $\widetilde\rho$ is the smooth lower clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}. To suppress spurious response to very small deviations we apply a smooth lazy-zone gate with half-width $\delta_0\ge 0$ (dimensionless):
\[
\delta_{\mathrm{eff}} \;:=\;
\begin{cases}
\delta\Big(1-\exp\!\big(-(|\delta|/\delta_0)^2\big)\Big), & \delta_0>0,\\
\delta, & \delta_0=0.
\end{cases}
\]
The mechanical drive $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ is constructed as a cycle-weighted power mean of the local strain energy density (SED) across a discrete set of loading cases:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-psi-nd}
\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x) \;=\;
\left(\frac{\sum_i c_i\,\psi_i(x)^p}{\sum_i c_i}\right)^{1/p},\qquad
\psi_i(x):=\max\!\Big(0,\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i(x):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i(x)\Big),
\end{equation}
where case weights $c_i$ are proportional to cycles-per-day (or relative daily frequency), and $p\ge 1$ biases the average toward peaks ($p=1$ gives a weighted mean, $p\to\infty$ approaches a max). The specific SED $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}/\widetilde\rho$ reflects sensing of strain energy per unit bone mass.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd} acts as a spatio-temporal low-pass filter of the mechanostat drive, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:nonlocal_stimulus}. Regions of sustained overload ($m>m_{\mathrm{ref}}$, i.e.\ $\delta>0$) accumulate positive stimulus, while underloaded regions ($\delta<0$) accumulate negative stimulus. Diffusion broadens and smooths these regions over the characteristic length
\(
\ell_S=\sqrt{\tau_S D_S}
\), while the reaction term damps transient fluctuations with a characteristic time
\(
t_S=\tau_S
\).
The time constant $\tau_S$ thus sets the inertia with respect to temporal fluctuations in the drive, while $D_S$ controls spatial spread via gap-junction/vascular pathways. The homogeneous Neumann condition corresponds to zero normal flux of $S$ at $\partial\Omega$.

\subsection*{Density submodel}\label{subsec:density-nd}
The apparent density $\rho=\rho(x,t)$ evolves under diffusive regularization and bounded formation/resorption kinetics driven by the stimulus $S$. The strong form is supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}
\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho\,\nabla\rho\big)\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
\rho(\cdot,0)=\rho_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\ \ \rho_{\min}\le \rho_0\le \rho_{\max}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Diffusive regularization.}
In this work, transport is modeled by an isotropic diffusion tensor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Drho-nd}
\mathbf{D}_\rho(x,t) \;=\; D_\rho\,\mathbf{I},
\end{equation}
with $D_\rho\ge 0$ in units mm$^2$/day. For later use (e.g.\ fabric-guided constitutive evaluation) we define a deviatoric (unit-determinant) normalization of $\mathbf{A}=\exp(\mathbf{L})$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}
\widehat{\mathbf{A}} \;:=\; \exp\!\Big(\mathbf{L}-\tfrac13\,\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{L})\,\mathbf{I}\Big),
\end{equation}
which is symmetric positive definite and satisfies $\det(\widehat{\mathbf{A}})=1$, i.e., it contains only the anisotropy (eigenvalue ratios) of $\mathbf{A}$.

\paragraph{Bounded formation/resorption kinetics.}
The density evolution is driven by the stimulus $S$ such that positive stimulus drives growth toward $\rho_{\max}$ and negative stimulus drives resorption toward $\rho_{\min}$. We use smooth positive/negative parts (consistent with the global smoothing width $\varepsilon$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smooth-splits-nd}
\operatorname{smoothmax}(a,b;\varepsilon):=\tfrac12\!\left(a+b+\sqrt{(a-b)^2+\varepsilon^2}-\varepsilon\right),\qquad
S_+(S):=\operatorname{smoothmax}(S,0;\varepsilon),\quad
S_-(S):=\operatorname{smoothmax}(-S,0;\varepsilon).
\end{equation}
The total evolution equation is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-strong-nd}
\partial_t \rho - \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}_\rho \nabla \rho) =
A_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho)\Big(
k_{\mathrm{form}}\,S_+(S)\Big(1-\frac{\rho}{\rho_{\max}}\Big)
\;+\;k_{\mathrm{res}}\,S_-(S)\Big(1-\frac{\rho}{\rho_{\min}}\Big)
\Big).
\end{equation}
Here $k_{\mathrm{form}},k_{\mathrm{res}}\ge 0$ are formation/resorption gains. The bracketed terms are soft bounds: as $\rho\to\rho_{\max}$ (formation) or $\rho\to\rho_{\min}$ (resorption) the corresponding rate vanishes, so $\rho$ remains naturally attracted to $[\rho_{\min},\rho_{\max}]$ without hard constraints. The optional surface-availability factor $A_{\mathrm{surf}}\in[A_{\min},1]$ scales activity based on a porosity/specific-surface proxy, which we evaluate explicitly at the previous time level.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:density-strong-nd} uses differentiable kinetics and soft bounds to provide a stable density update driven by the nonlocal stimulus field. The diffusion term $\nabla\!\cdot(\mathbf{D}_\rho\nabla\rho)$ introduces a spatial regularization length scale, preventing checkerboard patterns and promoting mesh-independent solutions. Here $\mathbf{D}_\rho$ is isotropic; fabric effects enter through the mechanics and the fabric evolution, and can be extended to anisotropic transport if desired.

\subsection*{Direction (fabric) submodel}\label{subsec:direction-nd}
The local fabric is described by a second-order, symmetric, positive-definite tensor field $\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}(x,t)$. To ensure positive definiteness and robustness, we evolve the logarithmic fabric tensor $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$. The dynamics follow a relaxation--diffusion flow toward a mechanically informed, trace-free logarithmic target $\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})$ defined below. The strong form on a bounded Lipschitz domain is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-strong-nd}
\begin{aligned}
c_A\,\partial_t \mathbf{L} \;-\; c_A D_A\,\Delta \mathbf{L} \;+\; \frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}
\;=\; \frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})\,,
\end{aligned}\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-bc-ic-nd}
\partial_{\mathbf{n}}\mathbf{L}:=\big(\nabla \mathbf{L}\big)\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{0}\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],
\qquad
\mathbf{L}(\cdot,0)=\ln(\mathbf{A}_0)\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $D_A\ge 0$ is a diffusion coefficient, $c_A>0$ a capacity (common scaling), and $\tau_A>0$ a relaxation time constant. The activity factor $a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})\in[0,1]$ suppresses updates under near-isotropic loading; the Laplacian and normal derivative act componentwise on $\mathbf{L}$.

\paragraph{Target construction from mechanics.}
For each loading case $i$, the mechanics solve provides $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i$ and $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i$. To extract \emph{directional} information and remove the hydrostatic contribution we form the symmetric deviatoric-stress outer-product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-def-nd}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}:=\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i-\tfrac13(\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i)\mathbf{I},\qquad
\mathbf{Q}_i \;:=\; \mathrm{sym}\!\big(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}^{\!\top}\big),
\end{equation}
and compute a cycle-weighted average
\(
\bar{\mathbf{Q}}:=\sum_i c_i \mathbf{Q}_i\big/\sum_i c_i
\),
where $c_i$ are the same daily weights as in \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}. To construct a bounded, trace-free logarithmic target we use the eigen-decomposition of a regularized tensor $\mathbf{Q}:=\mathrm{sym}(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})+\varepsilon_Q\mathbf{I}$, $\mathbf{Q}=\sum_i \lambda_i\mathbf{P}_i$, and define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-hat-nd}
\tilde m_i \;:=\; \operatorname{clamp}\!\big((\lambda_i/g)^{\gamma_F},\,m_{\min},\,m_{\max}\big),\qquad
g:=(\lambda_1\lambda_2\lambda_3)^{1/3},\qquad
\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})\;:=\;\sum_{i=1}^3\Big(\ln(\tilde m_i)-\tfrac13\sum_{j=1}^3\ln(\tilde m_j)\Big)\mathbf{P}_i,
\end{equation}
so that $\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{L}_M=0$ (equivalently $\det(\exp(\mathbf{L}_M))=1$) and the induced eigenvalue ratios are bounded by $m_{\min},m_{\max}$. Here $\operatorname{clamp}(x,a,b):=\min\{b,\max\{a,x\}\}$. In computation we evaluate $\{\lambda_i,\mathbf{P}_i\}$ using a stabilized Sylvester-projector construction (as above), which is robust under isotropic and transversely isotropic limits of $\mathbf{Q}$.

The activity factor $a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})$ is computed from the deviatoric magnitude of $\mathbf{Q}$: let $\mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{dev}}:=\mathbf{Q}-\frac13(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{Q})\mathbf{I}$ and
\[
r:=\frac{\sqrt{\mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{dev}}:\mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{dev}}+\varepsilon^2}}{\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{Q}+\varepsilon_Q},\qquad
a:=\frac{r}{r+\varepsilon_a}\in[0,1],
\]
with a small dimensionless $\varepsilon_a>0$.

\paragraph{Regularization parameters and defaults.}
We use a single global smoothing width $\varepsilon$ for the soft clamps in \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} and \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}. \subsection*{Gait Cycle Integration and Stimulus Accumulation}
The daily mechanical stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ aggregates the loading history over a full day. In our numerical experiments, the gait cycle is represented by a finite set of loading cases (phases) with relative daily frequencies $c_i$, and the daily driver is computed as the cycle-weighted power mean in \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}. The exponent $p$ plays a critical role in filtering the mechanical signal: $p=1$ gives a weighted mean, while larger $p$ increasingly biases toward peak loads ($p\to\infty$ approaches a max). Under linear elasticity $\psi_i\propto F^2$, so the power kernel scales as $F^{2p}$ and convexity (Jensen’s inequality) explains why dynamic peaks produce a stronger stimulus than a constant load of equivalent average magnitude.

\section*{Backward--Euler time discretization}
\label{sec:be-time}
Let $0=t^0<t^1<\dots<t^N=T$ be a partition with $\Delta t^n:=t^n-t^{n-1}>0$. For any time-dependent field $X$, write $X^n\approx X(\cdot,t^n)$. Mechanics is quasi-static (no time derivative); $S$, $\rho$, and $\mathbf{L}$ are advanced by fully implicit Backward--Euler steps. Boundary and initial conditions are enforced at each time level exactly as in \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}, \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}, and \eqref{eq:L-bc-ic-nd}.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$.}
Find $\mathbf{u}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-mech}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\exp(\mathbf{L}^{n})\right)=\mathbf{0}\ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}^{n}=\mathbf{0}\ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\exp(\mathbf{L}^{n})\right)\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \text{on }\Gamma_N,
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ given by the Zysset-Curnier model \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}.

\paragraph{Stimulus.}
Given $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}^{n}$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-stim}
\frac{\tau_S}{\Delta t^n}\big(S^{n}-S^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\tau_S D_S\,\Delta S^{n}\;+\;S^{n}
\;=\; S_{\max}\,\tanh\!\Big(\delta_{\mathrm{eff}}(\psi_{\mathrm{day}}^{n},\rho^{n})/\kappa_S\Big)\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the Neumann condition from \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd} and $S^{0}=S_0$.

\paragraph{Density.}
Let $\mathbf{D}_\rho$ be as in \eqref{eq:Drho-nd}. Find $\rho^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-rho}
\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\big(\rho^{n}-\rho^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho\,\nabla \rho^{n}\big)
\;=\; A_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho^{n-1})\left[
k_{\mathrm{form}}\,S_+^n\!\left(1-\frac{\rho^n}{\rho_{\max}}\right)
\;+\;k_{\mathrm{res}}\,S_-^n\!\left(1-\frac{\rho^n}{\rho_{\min}}\right)
\right]\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
Here $S_+^n:=S_+(S^n)$ and $S_-^n:=S_-(S^n)$ are the smooth positive/negative parts from \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}. We evaluate $A_{\mathrm{surf}}$ explicitly on $\rho^{n-1}$.

with the no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd} and $\rho^{0}=\rho_0$.

\paragraph{Direction/fabric.}
With $\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})$ given by \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, find $\mathbf{L}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-A}
\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\big(\mathbf{L}^{n}-\mathbf{L}^{n-1}\big)\;-\;c_A D_A\,\Delta \mathbf{L}^{n}\;+\;\frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}^{n}
\;=\; \frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the componentwise no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:L-bc-ic-nd} and $\mathbf{L}^{0}=\ln(\mathbf{A}_0)$.

\paragraph{Finite element spaces.}
For displacements we use the conforming space
\[
V_h:=\{\,\mathbf{v}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^d:\ \mathbf{v}_h=\mathbf{0}\text{ on }\Gamma_D\,\},
\]
for scalar fields $Q_h\subset H^1(\Omega)$, and for second-order tensors
\[
T_h:=\{\,\mathbf{Q}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^{d\times d}\,\}.
\]
We represent the tensor unknown $\mathbf{L}$ componentwise and symmetrize it after each fabric solve; the fabric tensor is recovered as $\mathbf{A}=\exp(\mathbf{L})$.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$ (weak form).}
Find $\mathbf{u}_h^{n}\in V_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{v}_h\in V_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-mech-unified}
a_u(\mathbf{u}_h^{n},\mathbf{v}_h;\rho^{n},\mathbf{L}^{n}) \;=\; \ell_u(\mathbf{v}_h),
\end{equation}
with the bilinear and linear forms
\begin{align*}
a_u(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{v};\rho,\mathbf{L})
&:= \int_\Omega \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{w}, \rho, \exp(\mathbf{L})) : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v}) \, \mathrm{d}x, \\[2mm]
\ell_u(\mathbf{v}) &:= \langle \mathbf{t},\mathbf{v} \rangle_{\Gamma_N},
\end{align*}
where $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is the Zysset-Curnier stress tensor defined in \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}.

\paragraph{Stimulus (Backward--Euler weak form).}
Given $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}^{n}$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $q_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-stim-unified}
\left(\left(\frac{\tau_S}{\Delta t^n}+1\right) S_h^{n}, q_h\right) \;+\; \tau_S D_S\,(\nabla S_h^{n}, \nabla q_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{\tau_S}{\Delta t^n} S_h^{n-1}, q_h\right) \;+\; \Big(S_{\max}\tanh\!\big(\delta_{\mathrm{eff}}(\psi_{\mathrm{day}}^{n},\rho^{n})/\kappa_S\big), q_h\Big).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Density (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{D}_\rho$ from \eqref{eq:Drho-nd}, find $\rho_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $p_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-rho-unified}
\begin{aligned}
\left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n}, p_h\right) \;+\; \big(\mathbf{D}_\rho\,\nabla \rho_h^{n}, \nabla p_h\big)
\;+\; \Big(A_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho_h^{n-1})\,\big(\tfrac{k_{\mathrm{form}}}{\rho_{\max}} S_+^n + \tfrac{k_{\mathrm{res}}}{\rho_{\min}} S_-^n\big)\,\rho_h^{n},\, p_h\Big) \\
\;=\; \left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n-1}, p_h\right) \;+\; \Big(A_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho_h^{n-1})\,(k_{\mathrm{form}} S_+^n + k_{\mathrm{res}} S_-^n),\, p_h\Big),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $S_+^n = S_+(S^n)$ and $S_-^n = S_-(S^n)$ with $S_\pm$ defined in \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}.

\paragraph{Direction/fabric (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})$ given by \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, find $\mathbf{L}_h^{n}\in T_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{Q}_h\in T_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-A-unified}
\left(\left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n} + \frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})}{\tau_A}\right)\mathbf{L}_h^{n}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; c_A D_A\,(\nabla \mathbf{L}_h^{n}, \nabla \mathbf{Q}_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\mathbf{L}_h^{n-1}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; \frac{c_A\,a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n})}{\tau_A}\,(\mathbf{L}_M(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{n}), \mathbf{Q}_h).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remarks.}
(i) Dirichlet conditions for mechanics are imposed strongly in $V_h$; natural boundary conditions enter via $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\Gamma_N}$. (ii) The tensor unknown $\mathbf{L}_h^{n}$ is represented componentwise; symmetry is preserved at the continuous level and is respected numerically. The fabric tensor is recovered as $\mathbf{A}^n = \exp(\mathbf{L}_h^n)$.

\section*{Fixed-point solver at a given time level: block Gauss--Seidel with Anderson acceleration}
\label{sec:fp-aa}
We describe the nonlinear solver used to obtain $(\mathbf{u}^n,S^n,\rho^n,\mathbf{L}^n)$ at a fixed time level $t^n$ from the Backward--Euler subproblems \eqref{eq:be-mech}--\eqref{eq:be-A}. Let the remodeling state be
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{X} \;:=\; T\times Q\times Q,\qquad
\mathbf{x} \;=\; (\mathbf{L},S,\rho) \in \mathcal{X},
\end{equation*}
with the product inner product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product-norm}
\langle \mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle_{\mathcal{X}} \;=\;
\alpha_L\,(\mathbf{L},\mathbf{Q}) \;+\; \alpha_S\,(S,q) \;+\; \alpha_\rho\,(\rho,p),
\end{equation}
for $\mathbf{y}=(\mathbf{Q},q,p)$ and positive weights $\alpha_\bullet$. The mechanics field $\mathbf{u}$ is treated as a dependent variable $\mathbf{u}(\rho,\mathbf{L})$ determined by the equilibrium equations. We monitor an RMS-per-DOF global $L^2$ norm on a scaled concatenation of $(\mathbf{L},S,\rho)$ (per-field scaling set from the first sweep); scalar/tensor blocks have no Dirichlet-constrained DOFs, so the “projection” is trivial.

\paragraph{Block map.}
We define the fixed-point iteration in terms of the mechanical drivers $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ (scalar stimulus driver) and $\bar{\mathbf{Q}}$ (cycle-averaged deviatoric-stress outer-product). Given the state $(\rho, \mathbf{L})$, the mechanics driver $\mathsf{U}$ determines equilibrium displacements for each loading case and generates
\[
\psi_{\mathrm{new}} = \psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\rho,\mathbf{L}), \qquad \bar{\mathbf{Q}}_{\mathrm{new}} = \bar{\mathbf{Q}}(\rho,\mathbf{L}).
\]
The block Gauss--Seidel map $\mathcal{G}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}$ is then defined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:block-map}
\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{L},S,\rho)
:=
\Big(\ \underbrace{\mathsf{L}(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}_{\mathrm{new}})}_{\text{fabric}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{S}(\psi_{\mathrm{new}},\rho)}_{\text{stim}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{R}(S_{\mathrm{new}})}_{\text{density}}\ \Big),
\end{equation}
where $S_{\mathrm{new}}=\mathsf{S}(\psi_{\mathrm{new}},\rho)$ is the updated stimulus. This structure highlights that $\mathsf{S}$ and $\mathsf{L}$ depend on mechanics only through intermediate drivers. We take the daily driver as a cycle-weighted power mean of local strain energy density across loading cases,
\[
\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x)=\left(\frac{\sum_i c_i\,\psi_i(x)^p}{\sum_i c_i}\right)^{1/p},\qquad
\psi_i(x):=\max\!\Big(0,\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i(x):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i(x)\Big),
\]
with exponent $p\ge 1$ and cycle weights $c_i$ (cycles/day); the fabric driver is $\bar{\mathbf{Q}}=\sum_i c_i\,\mathrm{sym}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}^{\!\top})/\sum_i c_i$ with $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i,\mathrm{dev}}:=\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i-\tfrac13(\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_i)\mathbf{I}$.

The fixed point at $t^n$ solves
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp-residual}
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}^n)\;:=\;\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^n)-\mathbf{x}^n \;=\; \mathbf{0}\quad\text{in }\mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block Gauss--Seidel iteration.}
Given the state $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}=(\mathbf{L}^{(m)},S^{(m)},\rho^{(m)})$, we first run the mechanics driver to evaluate the drivers $\psi=\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{L}^{(m)})$ and $\bar{\mathbf{Q}}=\bar{\mathbf{Q}}(\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{L}^{(m)})$. Then we perform the updates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gs-sweep}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{L}^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{L}\big(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\big),\\
S^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{S}\big(\psi,\rho^{(m)}\big),\\
\rho^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{R}\big(S^{(m+1)}\big).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The sweep \eqref{eq:gs-sweep} defines the raw update $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}:=\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^{(m)})$. No external scalar damping $\omega_m$ is used; instead, damping/backtracking are handled inside the accelerator (see below).

\paragraph{Anderson acceleration (Type~II with $\sum\alpha=1$).}
We use a global, MPI-aware Type~II Anderson (Pulay) accelerator built on \emph{residual} snapshots
\(
r^{(j)}:=x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(j)}-\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}
\)
and corresponding states $\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}$. With window length $p$ (capped by $m+1$), form the residual stack $R=[r^{(m-p+2)},\dots,r^{(m+1)}]^\top$ and the \emph{global} Gram matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-gram}
H \;:=\; R\,R^\top \quad(\text{reduced across MPI ranks}),
\end{equation}
then solve the equality--constrained, Tikhonov-regularized system for the weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in\mathbb{R}^p$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kkt}
\begin{bmatrix} H + \lambda_{\mathrm{AA}} I & \mathbf{1} \\ \mathbf{1}^\top & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\alpha} \\ \eta \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{0} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\qquad
\lambda_{\mathrm{AA}} \;=\; \lambda\,\max\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{tr}(H)}{p},\,1\right).
\end{equation}
If $p\ge 2$, we apply a cheap residual-energy safeguard using the Gram matrix: with $r^2_{\mathrm{curr}}:=H_{p,p}$ and $r^2_{\mathrm{pred}}:=\boldsymbol{\alpha}^\top H\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}$, we reject the accelerated combination if $r^2_{\mathrm{pred}}>(1-\gamma)^2\,r^2_{\mathrm{curr}}$ (or if $r^2_{\mathrm{pred}}$ is non-finite) and instead take the (possibly damped) Picard step $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}+\beta\,r^{(m+1)}$. Otherwise (or if $p<2$), the accelerated candidate is assembled as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-update}
\mathbf{y} \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \alpha_j\Big(\mathbf{x}^{(m-p+j)} + \beta\,r^{(m-p+j+1)}\Big),\qquad
\mathbf{s}:=\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)},\qquad \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}:=\mathbf{x}^{(m)}+\mathbf{s},
\end{equation}
with a \emph{step limiter} enforcing $\|\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}} - \mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}} \le c_{\mathrm{step}}\,\|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)} - \mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}$ (implemented by scaling $\mathbf{s}$ if needed).

\paragraph{Projected-residual merit, safeguard, and backtracking.}
The acceptance test uses an \emph{RMS-per-DOF} 2--norm on the scaled flattened state $(\mathbf{L},S,\rho)$ (blockwise scaled by the first-sweep RMS and by $1/\sqrt{N_i}$). For these blocks there are no Dirichlet constraints, so the projection is the identity. With
\(
r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)} := \frac{\|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}}{\|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}}
\)
and the \emph{proxy} for a candidate $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ given by
\(
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} := \frac{\|\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}}{\|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}},
\)
we accept the Anderson step iff
\begin{equation}\label{eq:safeguard}
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} \;\le\; (1-\gamma)\,r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}.
\end{equation}
Otherwise we backtrack along $\mathbf{s}$ by halving (up to $N_{\mathrm{bt}}$ attempts) until \eqref{eq:safeguard} holds; if backtracking fails, we \emph{fall back} to the (possibly damped) Picard step $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}+\beta\,(x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)})$. This safeguard is \emph{cheap}: it requires no extra GS sweep because the proxy only uses differences of already known states.

\paragraph{Automatic restarts and conditioning.}
The accelerator maintains the best Picard merit seen so far and schedules a history reset (on the \emph{next} call) if any of the following triggers fire: (i) a streak of $\ge k_{\mathrm{rej}}$ consecutive AA rejections; (ii) Picard merit stalls, i.e., $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}>\chi_{\mathrm{stall}}\,r_{\mathrm{best}}$ with $r_{\mathrm{best}}$ the best Picard merit so far; (iii) the estimated condition number of $H+\lambda_{\mathrm{AA}}I$ exceeds $\kappa_{\max}$. In case of linear-algebra failure in \eqref{eq:kkt}, uniform weights are used as a safe fallback. No preconditioner-driven resets are used; preconditioner maintenance (if any) is decoupled from Anderson.

\paragraph{Stopping and logging.}
We monitor the projected Picard residual $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}$ itself and stop once $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}<\mathrm{tol}$ and at least a user-specified minimum number of subiterations has been taken. The Gram-matrix prediction check, safeguard/backtracking \eqref{eq:safeguard}, and step limiting ensure that any accepted Anderson update has a proxy step no worse than the contemporaneous Picard update in this metric. A separate \emph{blockwise relative iterate change} and Anderson diagnostics (history depth, conditioning, restarts) are recorded for analysis but are not used for stopping.

\paragraph{Well-posedness of subsolves.}
At each evaluation of $\mathcal{G}$ the four subproblems are uniformly elliptic (for fixed arguments) and admit unique solutions in the chosen finite-dimensional spaces: (i) mechanics has a coercive bilinear form by the density floor and $\nu\in(-1,\tfrac12)$, (ii) stimulus is SPD by $(\tau_S/\Delta t^n+1)$ with $D_S\ge0$, (iii) density is SPD by $(1/\Delta t^n)+A_{\mathrm{surf}}(\rho^{n-1})\big(k_{\mathrm{form}}S_+/\rho_{\max}+k_{\mathrm{res}}S_-/\rho_{\min}\big)\ge 1/\Delta t^n$ together with $\mathbf{D}_\rho=D_\rho\mathbf{I}$, and (iv) fabric is SPD by $(c_A/\Delta t^n)+c_A a(\bar{\mathbf{Q}})/\tau_A>0$ with $D_A\ge 0$. Thus each application of $\mathcal{G}$ is well-defined.

\paragraph{Convergence discussion.}
On bounded sets where the smooth gates/clamps are Lipschitz, $\mathcal{G}$ is locally Lipschitz. If the induced modulus $L<1$, the undamped Gauss--Seidel iteration is linearly convergent. The safeguard \eqref{eq:safeguard} enforces a strict improvement of the accepted AA proxy step relative to the contemporaneous Picard step (by factor $1-\gamma$); combined with step limiting and automatic restarts, this yields robust global behavior of the AA-wrapped method even when the least-squares system \eqref{eq:kkt} is poorly conditioned.

\paragraph{Algorithm (per time level $t^n$).}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Initialize $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}$ (from $t^{n-1}$ or a warm start) and the AA history; set counters/timers to zero.
\item Perform one GS sweep to obtain $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$ and set $\mathbf{x}^{(1)}\!\leftarrow\!x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$.
\item For $m=1,2,\dots$ until the stopping test holds:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform one GS sweep to get $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$.
  \item If AA is enabled, form $H$ from residual histories, solve \eqref{eq:kkt}, assemble $\mathbf{y}$, apply step limiting, and build $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$.
  \item Evaluate the projected-residual merit and apply the safeguard/backtracking \eqref{eq:safeguard}; fall back to Picard if needed.
  \item Accept the step, update histories (and schedule a restart if a trigger fires), and log diagnostics.
  \end{enumerate}
\item Output $\mathbf{x}^{n}:=\mathbf{x}^{(m_\star)}$ for the first index $m_\star$ meeting the stopping criterion.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Physical Parameter Analysis and Model Calibration}

We adopt a consistent set of physical parameters for both the full-scale femur simulations and the representative volume element (RVE) verification tests. Unless stated otherwise, we report length in [mm], time in [day], stress/energy in [MPa], and density in [g/cm$^3$].

\paragraph{Mechanical (elasticity) parameters.}
The density--elasticity law uses $E_0=7500\,\mathrm{MPa}$ ($7.5\,\mathrm{GPa}$), $\nu_0=0.3$, and a smooth exponent transition from $n_{\mathrm{trab}}=2.0$ to $n_{\mathrm{cort}}=1.3$ over the density interval $[\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max},\rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}]$ with $\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max}=1.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}=1.25\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$. We use the reference density $\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}=1.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ in the power law and the smooth lower clamp $\rho_{\min}=0.1\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$. Fabric-guided directional scaling uses stiffness exponents $p_E=1.0$ (axial) and $p_G=1.0$ (shear), with eigenvalue-ratio bounds $m_{\min}=0.2$ and $m_{\max}=5.0$.

\paragraph{Stimulus (mechanoregulation) parameters.}
The cycle-weighted power mean in \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd} uses exponent $p=4.0$. The mechanostat is parametrized by $\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}=0.01\,\mathrm{MPa}$ and the specific-energy reference $m_{\mathrm{ref}}=\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}/\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}$ with $\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}=1.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$. The diffusion--reaction constants are $\tau_S=25\,\mathrm{day}$ and $D_S=1.0\,\mathrm{mm^2/day}$, and the dimensionless saturation/lazy-zone parameters are $S_{\max}=1.0$, $\kappa_S=0.5$, and $\delta_0=0.10$.

\paragraph{Density-evolution parameters.}
The density bounds and initialization are $\rho_{\min}=0.1\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$, $\rho_{\max}=2.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$, and $\rho_0=1.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$, with reference density $\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}=1.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$. Formation and resorption gains are $k_{\mathrm{form}}=k_{\mathrm{res}}=2\times 10^{-2}\,\mathrm{g/cm^3/day}$, and the isotropic density diffusion coefficient is $D_\rho=2\times 10^{-2}\,\mathrm{mm^2/day}$. The surface-availability factor $A_{\mathrm{surf}}$ is enabled in the results below and uses $\rho_{\mathrm{tissue}}=2.0\,\mathrm{g/cm^3}$, $A_{\min}=0.02$ (dimensionless), and $S_0=1.0\,\mathrm{mm^{-1}}$.

\paragraph{Fabric-evolution parameters.}
The log-fabric equation \eqref{eq:L-strong-nd} uses $\tau_A=50\,\mathrm{day}$, $D_A=1.0\,\mathrm{mm^2/day}$, and capacity $c_A=1.0$ (dimensionless). The target construction \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd} uses exponent $\gamma_F=1.0$, diagonal regularization $\varepsilon_Q=10^{-12}\,\mathrm{MPa^2}$ (added to $\bar{\mathbf Q}$), and eigenvalue-ratio bounds $m_{\min}=0.2$ and $m_{\max}=5.0$. The activity gate uses $\varepsilon_a=10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Linear and coupling solver parameters.}
Each linear block solve uses MINRES with GAMG preconditioning and tolerances $\mathrm{rtol}=10^{-6}$, $\mathrm{atol}=10^{-7}$, and $\mathrm{it}_{\max}=100$; we reuse the Krylov solver object between sweeps and do not force preconditioner reuse between successive assemblies. Nonlinear coupling at a fixed time level uses block Gauss--Seidel with Anderson acceleration (enabled in all results below): history length $m=5$, mixing $\beta=1.0$, regularization $\lambda=10^{-9}$, safeguard threshold $\gamma=0.05$, backtracking cap $N_{\mathrm{bt}}=5$, and step-limit factor $c_{\mathrm{step}}=2.0$. Restarts trigger for $k_{\mathrm{rej}}=2$ consecutive rejections, stall ratio $\chi_{\mathrm{stall}}=1.10$, and conditioning threshold $\kappa_{\max}=10^{12}$. The stopping criterion uses $\mathrm{tol}=10^{-4}$ with $N_{\min}=2$ and $N_{\max}=25$ subiterations.

\paragraph{Time stepping and numerics.}
We simulate up to $T=500\,\mathrm{day}$ with constant timestep $\Delta t=25\,\mathrm{day}$. Optional adaptive stepping (AB2 predictor with a Gustafsson PI controller; disabled in the results below) is parametrized by tolerances $\mathrm{rtol}_t=10^{-2}$ and $\mathrm{atol}_t=10^{-3}$, bounds $\Delta t_{\min}=10^{-4}\,\mathrm{day}$ and $\Delta t_{\max}=100\,\mathrm{day}$, and PI parameters $s=0.9$, $g_{\max}=5.0$, $g_{\min}=0.1$, $k_{\mathrm{exp}}=1.5$, $k_P=0.20$, and $k_I=0.40$. Smooth clamps/splits use a global smoothing width $\varepsilon=10^{-6}$ (interpreted in the units of the smoothed quantity), and numerical integration uses quadrature degree $q=4$.

\paragraph{Output and mesh tags.}
We write outputs every $N_{\mathrm{save}}=1$ accepted timesteps to an output directory \texttt{.results} with log file \texttt{simulation.log}. The Dirichlet and Neumann boundary parts $(\Gamma_D,\Gamma_N)$ are defined by mesh facet tags with IDs $(i_D,i_N)=(1,2)$.

\paragraph{Calibration for Convergence Studies.}
For the spatial convergence analysis presented in \ref{app:convergence}, we employ a cubic control volume of side length $L=5\,\mathrm{mm}$. The material parameters match the baseline set above. Loading is applied via Periodic (PMUBC) or Kinematic (KUBC) boundary conditions to generate an apparent strain of magnitude $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}=10^{-3}$, ensuring the response remains within the linear elastic regime.

\subsection*{Preconditioner maintenance}\label{subsec:ksp_defaults}
Each subsolver keeps a single PETSc KSP object that is reused as $(\rho,\mathbf{L},S)$ evolve, with the operator updated after each assembly. Preconditioner setup therefore follows PETSc's standard reuse policy and is decoupled from the AA/GS logic.
