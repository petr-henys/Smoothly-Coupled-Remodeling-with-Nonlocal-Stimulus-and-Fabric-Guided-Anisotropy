\begin{abstract}
We propose a four-field bone-remodeling formulation that co-evolves displacement $\mathbf{u}$, apparent density $\rho$, log-fabric $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$, and a nonlocal mechanotransductive stimulus $S$. The elastic response follows the Zysset-Curnier orthotropic model, where stiffness tensors are constructed from the spectral decomposition of the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$. The stimulus follows a diffusion--reaction balance driven by elastic energy; density evolves via anisotropic transport and a continuous thermodynamic driver $\dot{\rho} \propto S$; fabric anisotropy evolves on the logarithmic manifold, relaxing toward a trace-safe normalization of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ with spatial diffusion. Time integration is fully implicit Backward--Euler; space is discretized by conforming $H^1$ finite elements in a unified weak-form notation. Nonlinear coupling is solved by a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel sweep (mechanics $\rightarrow$ stimulus $\rightarrow$ density $\rightarrow$ fabric) wrapped in Type-II Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and residual safeguards. A single smoothing policy used across clamping and normalizations ensures differentiability and preserves coercivity, while the solver architecture yields stable, mesh-robust convergence without monolithic Jacobians.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
\label{s:introduction}
Bone is a living composite that continually reorganizes its microstructure to balance mechanical function with metabolic cost. Four mechanobiologically distinct processes drive this adaptation: (i) the elastic response of the solid matrix to loads, (ii) the evolution of mass (apparent density/porosity), (iii) the reorientation of the trabecular/collagen network (fabric anisotropy), and (iv) the production and spatial transport of cell-mediated biochemical signals that couple local mechanics to osteoblast/osteoclast activity. Robust numerical prediction of morphology and stiffness therefore requires a model that co-evolves these \emph{four} fields---displacement $u$, density $\rho$, fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$, and a diffusive mechanotransductive stimulus $S$---within a single, tightly coupled formulation.

Mechanobiology supports this coupling. First, the mechanical field is not merely a passive input: Turner’s “three rules” assert that the magnitude, rate (frequency), and rest intervals of \emph{dynamic} loading govern the osteogenic response \cite{Turner1998_ThreeRulesBone}. Spatial \emph{strain gradients}---proxies for lacuno--canalicular (LCN) fluid flow---localize bone formation more reliably than strain magnitude alone, as shown in turkey radius models \cite{Gross1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate} and in exercise-induced periosteal activation in rooster models \cite{Zernicke1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate}. These gradients act through osteocytes embedded in the LCN, where fluid shear induces Ca$^{2+}$/Wnt signaling; acute loading suppresses sclerostin (Sost), while unloading elevates it, shifting the local formation/resorption balance \cite{Robling2008_MechanicalStimulationBone}. While mechanosensitive channels such as PIEZO1 contribute to skeletal homeostasis, their cell-type-specific roles in load adaptation are nuanced and do not obviate the need to resolve the full biophysical cascade from matrix strains to fluid shear to osteocytic signaling \cite{Wang2020_MechanicalSensingProtein}. Together, these findings motivate an explicit stimulus field $S$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical cues and \emph{transported} over osteocytic/vascular pathways (e.g., via diffusion--decay dynamics), rather than a purely local, algebraic “mechanical index.”

Second, accurate mechanics--morphology feedback requires density $\rho$ and anisotropy $\mathbf{A}$ to co-evolve and feed back into the elastic moduli. Empirically, bone volume fraction (BV/TV) explains most variance in cancellous stiffness, and fabric anisotropy accounts for nearly all of the remainder; together they reach $\sim$95--98\% explanatory power across anatomical sites, with negligible contribution from other morphometric covariates \cite{Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Theoretical and constitutive links between $\{\rho,\mathbf{A}\}$ and orthotropic elastic constants are well established---Cowin’s fabric--elasticity relations (1985, 1990) and the Zysset--Curnier orthotropic model (1995) connect BV/TV and second-order fabric tensors to the apparent stiffness tensor (including $E$, $G$, and $\nu$) \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}. In practice, fabric can be quantified directly from imaging using gray-level structure-tensor or mean-intercept-length methods and validated on HR-pQCT/$\mu$CT, enabling evolution laws for $\mathbf{A}$ aligned with principal stress/flow directions \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Ali2017_Mu, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}. As $\rho$ and $\mathbf{A}$ change through remodeling, the spatial stiffness field---and thus the displacement field $u$ and its gradients---must be updated consistently to avoid spurious transients and mesh-dependent artifacts; morphology--elasticity models with full fabric information achieve the highest predictive accuracy in such coupled simulations \cite{Schneider2012_MorphologyElasticityRelationships, Panyasantis2016_EffectiveElasticProperties}.

Third, the remodeling stimulus that drives BMU-scale biology is not localized to a voxel or finite element. Experiments and theory both indicate spatial nonlocality through osteocytic/vascular networks; modern formulations therefore promote the stimulus to a \emph{field} obeying a diffusion--reaction equation, which systematically removes unphysical checkerboarding and captures healing or damage--remodeling interactions \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. Recent work also explores multi-stimulus energetics (e.g., shear vs.\ normal components) in continuum settings, reinforcing the need to track multiple evolving material parameters beyond a single density variable \cite{Branecka2024_BoneRemodelingModel}.

From a numerical standpoint, loosely coupled or explicit updates are in general more fragile than monolithic schemes: density-only algorithms are prone to mesh-dependent checkerboards and near-field oscillations near load singularities \cite{Jacobs1995_NumericalInstabilitiesBone, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. We therefore retain a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel fixed point and wrap it in Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and safeguards; see the solver description in \S\ref{sec:fp-aa}. This combination substantially mitigates splitting artifacts and yields robust, quasi mesh-independent convergence in our experiments \cite{Both2019_AndersonAcceleratedFixed, Walker2011_AndersonAcceleration, Fang2009_TwoClassesMultisecant} while remaining minimally invasive on top of standard FE components.

\section*{Comparison with related models and novelty}
\label{sec:related-novelty}
Our formulation sits between classical density--only mechanostat models and fully coupled cell--population or damage--remodeling frameworks. In \emph{local} mechanostat models the remodeling drive is a pointwise function of a mechanical index, typically strain or strain--energy density, and only the density $\rho$ evolves; transport and fabric are absent and spatial regularity is supplied ad hoc (cf.\ early density models and follow--ups reviewed in \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological}). Nonlocal and \emph{diffusive} stimulus fields---as in \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd}---have been advocated to account for osteocytic/vascular communication and to remove checkerboarding; this strand has gained traction in the last decade \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach}. On the elasticity side, orthotropic models that couple BV/TV and second--order fabric tensors to the full fourth--order stiffness tensor (Cowin's relations and the Zysset--Curnier model) are now standard in homogenized micro--FE and hFE pipelines \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic, Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Our constitutive choice preserves this spirit but adopts an energetically nonnegative, rank--one reinforcement aligned with a unit--trace direction proxy $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$, which empirically captures the dominant directional effect while simplifying the numerics; it leaves the isotropic floor strictly coercive through the smooth clamp $\widetilde\rho\ge\rho_{\min}$, cf.\ \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}.

In the \emph{evolution} of anisotropy, several authors align fabric with stress or strain measures and regularize it by diffusion or smoothing (e.g.\ structure--tensor approaches and stress--alignment hypotheses, see \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}). We follow this line by relaxing $\mathbf{A}$ toward the trace--safe normalization $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$ of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, which robustly aligns principal directions while avoiding non--differentiable spectral projections; positive definiteness of $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ is enforced via the Gram normalisation with $\varepsilon>0$ (the isotropic fallback and transport floor suffice in practice).

\section*{Governing equations}
\label{sec:strong-forms-nd}
\paragraph{Units and scaling.}
The reference implementation solves the governing equations in dimensional form using the unit system: length [mm], time [day], mass [tonne], and stress/energy [MPa]. The stimulus $S$ and relative density $\rho$ are dimensionless fields, while fabric $\mathbf{A}$ is dimensionless. The coefficients $c_S, \kappa_S, \tau_S$ carry appropriate physical units to ensure dimensional consistency. The boundary partition is $\partial\Omega=\Gamma_D\cup\Gamma_N$ with outward unit normal $\mathbf{n}$.

\subsection*{Mechanical submodel}
\label{subsec:mech-nd}
\paragraph{Kinematics and equilibrium.}
We adopt small-strain kinematics with the symmetric gradient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-eps-nd}
\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}) := \tfrac12\big(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^\top\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}_{\rm sym},\qquad d\in\{2,3\}.
\end{equation}
Under quasi-static conditions the balance of linear momentum reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-strong-nd}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_N.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Constitutive structure.}
We adopt the Zysset-Curnier orthotropic material model \cite{Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}, which defines the stiffness tensor $\mathbb{C}$ based on the apparent density $\rho$ and the eigenvalues $m_1, m_2, m_3$ of the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$. The fabric tensor is recovered from the logarithmic field $\mathbf{L}$ via the matrix exponential $\mathbf{A} = \exp(\mathbf{L})$.
The orthotropic engineering constants are given by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:zysset-curnier}
\begin{aligned}
    E_i &= E_0 \, \widetilde{\rho}^k \, m_i^p, \\
    G_{ij} &= G_0 \, \widetilde{\rho}^k \, (m_i m_j)^{p/2}, \\
    \nu_{ij} &= \nu_0,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $E_0, G_0, \nu_0$ are reference moduli, $k$ is the density exponent, and $p$ is the fabric exponent. The density $\widetilde{\rho}$ is smoothly clamped to a minimum value $\rho_{\min}$ to ensure positive definiteness. The stress is then $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathbb{C} : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$.

\paragraph{Smooth lower density clamp.}
To maintain uniform strong ellipticity and avoid case distinctions in highly porous regions, we use the same smooth ``max'' as in the implementation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smooth-max-nd}
\widetilde{\rho}(\rho;\rho_{\min},\varepsilon)
:=\rho_{\min}+\tfrac12\!\left[(\rho-\rho_{\min})+\sqrt{(\rho-\rho_{\min})^2+\varepsilon^2}\right].
\end{equation}
This monotone, everywhere differentiable approximation of $\max\{\rho,\rho_{\min}\}$ enforces $\widetilde{\rho}\ge\rho_{\min}$ with a single global smoothing width $\varepsilon>0$. No upper clamp is imposed.

\paragraph{Log-Euclidean Fabric Framework.}
To ensure the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$ remains symmetric and positive-definite (SPD) throughout the simulation, we evolve its matrix logarithm $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$. The fabric tensor is recovered via $\mathbf{A} = \exp(\mathbf{L})$. This parameterization guarantees that $\mathbf{A}$ is always SPD for any real symmetric tensor $\mathbf{L}$, avoiding the need for ad-hoc projections or constraints.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Figure \ref{fig:constitutive_law} illustrates the behavior of the density-elasticity law. Panel (a) shows the stiffness $E(\rho)$ transitioning from a quadratic trabecular regime ($n_{\mathrm{trab}}=2.0$) to a near-linear cortical regime ($n_{\mathrm{cort}}=1.2$). This dual-power law captures the stiffening efficiency of dense bone while maintaining the rapid compliance increase in porous bone. Panel (b) explicitly plots the exponent $n(\rho)$, which varies smoothly between the two regimes via the cubic smoothstep function shown in (f). Panels (c) and (d) demonstrate the sensitivity of the stiffness curve to the transition thresholds $\rho_{\mathrm{trab}}^{\max}$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{cort}}^{\min}$; shifting these parameters allows for calibration to specific anatomical sites where the trabecular-cortical boundary may differ. Panel (e) highlights the effect of the exponents themselves; higher $n_{\mathrm{trab}}$ suppresses stiffness in low-density regions, effectively modeling the loss of connectivity in osteoporotic bone. The smooth lower clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} guarantees strictly positive stiffness even in highly porous regions, thus preventing mechanical degeneracy and preserving differentiability. The normalization \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} filters the computed direction field into a \emph{symmetric, unit-trace} descriptor; with $\varepsilon>0$ it is \emph{positive-definite}. This combination yields a mechanically transparent, numerically robust model suitable for coupling with remodeling stimuli and evolution equations.

\subsection*{Stimulus submodel}\label{subsec:stim-nd}
We represent the mechanotransductive drive by a scalar field $S:\Omega\times(0,T]\to\mathbb{R}$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical activity and \emph{transported} through osteocytic/vascular pathways. Positive values correspond to net formation drive and negative values to net resorption drive.

The stimulus obeys a linear diffusion--reaction balance driven by the deviation of a daily mechanical stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ from a homeostatic reference level of $1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-strong-nd}
c_S\,\partial_t S \;-\; \kappa_S\,\Delta S \;+\; \tau_S\,S
\;=\; r_S\big(\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A}) - 1\big)
\quad\text{in }\Omega\times(0,T],
\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous Neumann boundary conditions and an initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}
\nabla S\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\ \ \text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
S(\cdot,0)=S_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $c_S>0$ denotes a temporal capacity, $\kappa_S\ge 0$ a diffusivity, and $\tau_S>0$ the reaction/turnover rate. The mechanical drive $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ is a cycle-averaged, normalized stress measure (Carter--Beaupr\'e type). For a gait cycle with $N_{\mathrm{cyc}}$ cycles per day, period $T_{\mathrm{cyc}}$, and weighting $w(t)$, it is defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-psi-nd}
\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x) \;=\; N_{\mathrm{cyc}} \int_0^{T_{\mathrm{cyc}}} w(t) \left( \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{vm}}(x,t)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right)^m \,\frac{\mathrm{d}t}{T_{\mathrm{cyc}}},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{vm}}$ is the Von Mises equivalent stress, $\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is a reference stress (e.g., $20\,\mathrm{MPa}$), and $m$ is an empirical exponent.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd} acts as a spatio-temporal low-pass filter of the mechanical drive $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}-1$, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:nonlocal_stimulus}. Regions of sustained overload ($\psi_{\mathrm{day}}>1$) accumulate positive stimulus, while underloaded regions accumulate negative stimulus. Diffusion broadens and smooths these regions over the characteristic length
\(
\ell_S=\sqrt{\kappa_S/\tau_S}
\), while the reaction term damps transient fluctuations with a characteristic time
\(
t_S=c_S/\tau_S
\).
The capacity $c_S$ thus sets the inertia with respect to temporal fluctuations in the drive, $\kappa_S$ controls spatial spread via gap-junction/vascular pathways, and $\tau_S$ sets the local turnover toward homeostasis. The homogeneous Neumann condition corresponds to zero normal flux of $S$ at $\partial\Omega$.

\subsection*{Density submodel}\label{subsec:density-nd}
The apparent density $\rho=\rho(x,t)$ evolves under anisotropic transport aligned with the fabric and a linear thermodynamic driver. The strong form is supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}
\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho\,\nabla\rho\big)\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
\rho(\cdot,0)=\rho_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\ \ \rho_{\min}\le \rho_0\le \rho_{\max}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Anisotropic transport.}
Directional transport is modeled by the symmetric diffusion tensor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Drho-nd}
\mathbf{D}_\rho(x,t) \;=\; \beta_{\perp}\,\mathbf{I} \;+\; \big(\beta_{\parallel}-\beta_{\perp}\big)\,\widehat{\mathbf{A}}(x,t),
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{\parallel},\beta_{\perp}>0$ are diffusivities along and transverse to the fabric, respectively. Here $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ is the unit-trace normalization of the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A} = \exp(\mathbf{L})$, ensuring that diffusion is guided by the principal directions of the microstructure.

\paragraph{Linear thermodynamic driver.}
We replace the discontinuous mechanostat with a continuous, thermodynamically consistent driver. The density evolution is driven by the stimulus $S$ such that positive stimulus drives growth toward $\rho_{\max}$ and negative stimulus drives resorption toward $\rho_{\min}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear-driver}
\dot{\rho}_{\mathrm{react}} = k_\rho \left[ \mathcal{H}(S)(\rho_{\max} - \rho) + \mathcal{H}(-S)(\rho_{\min} - \rho) \right],
\end{equation}
where $k_\rho$ is a rate constant and $\mathcal{H}(\cdot)$ is a smooth approximation of the Heaviside step function (implemented as $\text{pos}(S) = \max(0, S)$). This formulation ensures that density remains naturally bounded within $[\rho_{\min}, \rho_{\max}]$ without the need for hard constraints, as the driving force vanishes at the boundaries. The total evolution equation is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-strong-nd}
\partial_t \rho - \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}_\rho \nabla \rho) = k_\rho \left[ S^+ (\rho_{\max} - \rho) + S^- (\rho_{\min} - \rho) \right],
\end{equation}
where $S^+ = \max(0, S)$ and $S^- = \max(0, -S)$.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:density-strong-nd} represents a significant departure from discontinuous mechanostat models. Instead of a "lazy zone" with sharp thresholds, the linear thermodynamic driver provides a continuous response to mechanical stimulus, where the remodeling rate is proportional to the stimulus magnitude and the distance to the saturation limits. The terms $(\rho_{\max} - \rho)$ and $(\rho_{\min} - \rho)$ act as soft variational clamps, ensuring that the density remains strictly bounded within $[\rho_{\min}, \rho_{\max}]$ without imposing artificial hard constraints. As $\rho \to \rho_{\max}$, the growth rate naturally vanishes, and similarly for resorption as $\rho \to \rho_{\min}$. This formulation improves numerical stability and differentiability, facilitating the use of Newton-type solvers. The diffusion term $\nabla\!\cdot(\mathbf{D}_\rho\nabla\rho)$ introduces a spatial regularization length scale, preventing the formation of checkerboard patterns and ensuring mesh-independent solutions. The anisotropy of $\mathbf{D}_\rho$, guided by the fabric tensor $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$, promotes the formation of continuous trabecular structures aligned with the principal stress trajectories.

\subsection*{Direction (fabric) submodel}\label{subsec:direction-nd}
The local fabric is described by a second-order, symmetric, positive-definite tensor field $\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}(x,t)$. To ensure positive definiteness and robustness, we evolve the logarithmic fabric tensor $\mathbf{L} = \ln \mathbf{A}$. The dynamics follow a relaxation--diffusion flow toward a logarithmic target $\mathbf{L}_M = \ln \widehat{\mathbf{M}}$. The strong form on a bounded Lipschitz domain is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-strong-nd}
\begin{aligned}
c_A\,\partial_t \mathbf{L} \;-\; \frac{c_A\,\ell^2}{\tau_A}\,\Delta \mathbf{L} \;+\; \frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}
\;=\; \frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\,\ln(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u})) \,.
\end{aligned}\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-bc-ic-nd}
\partial_{\mathbf{n}}\mathbf{L}:=\big(\nabla \mathbf{L}\big)\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{0}\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],
\qquad
\mathbf{L}(\cdot,0)=\ln(\mathbf{A}_0)\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $\ell>0$ is an interaction length, $c_A>0$ a capacity, and $\tau_A>0$ a relaxation time constant. The Laplacian and normal derivative act componentwise on $\mathbf{L}$.

\paragraph{Target construction from mechanics.}
Let $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})$ be the small-strain tensor from the mechanical submodel and $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{dev}} = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - \frac{1}{3}(\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})\mathbf{I}$ be its deviatoric part. The instantaneous, dimensionless alignment tensor is defined by the deviatoric strain covariance:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-def-nd}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{u}) \;:=\; \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{dev}}(\mathbf{u})^\top\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{dev}}(\mathbf{u}).
\end{equation}
We normalize it by the same \emph{trace-safe shift-and-rescale} used throughout (no blending or gating),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-hat-nd}
\widehat{\mathbf{M}} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{M}+\varepsilon_M\,\mathbf{I}}{\mathrm{tr}\!\big(\mathbf{M}+\varepsilon_M\,\mathbf{I}\big)}\,,
\end{equation}
which is smooth in $\mathbf{M}$, symmetric, \emph{positive-definite for $\varepsilon_M>0$}, and unit-trace by construction. In the gait-driven context, $\mathbf{M}$ is averaged over the loading cycle.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Figure \ref{fig:anisotropy_law} visualizes the fabric-dependent properties. Panel (a) presents polar plots of the directional stiffness $C(\theta)$ for isotropic, uniaxial, and shear fabric states. The uniaxial case shows a pronounced stiffening along the principal axis, while the shear case exhibits a rotated symmetry. Panel (b) shows the corresponding directional diffusability $D(\theta)$, which facilitates mass transport along the trabecular grain. Panel (c) demonstrates how the anisotropy factor $\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}$ controls the magnitude of the directional reinforcement; higher $\xi$ leads to a more eccentric stiffness profile. Panel (d) shows the effect of the transverse diffusivity ratio $\beta_{\perp}/\beta_{\parallel}$ on the transport anisotropy. Panel (e) plots the reinforcement factor $(\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{n})^2$, which is the geometric driver of the stiffness increase. Finally, Panel (f) maps the fabric tensors into the eigenvalue space of unit-trace tensors (Barycentric coordinates), illustrating the feasible region for fabric states ranging from isotropic (center) to perfectly uniaxial (corners). The reaction term $\frac{c_A}{\tau_A}(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}-\mathbf{A})$ in \eqref{eq:A-strong-nd} drives $\mathbf{A}$ toward the mechanically informed, unit-trace target, while the diffusion $-\frac{c_A \ell^2}{\tau_A}\Delta\mathbf{A}$ spreads directional information and regularizes spatial oscillations. The homogeneous Neumann boundary condition represents no flux of directional content across $\partial\Omega$. Symmetry is preserved by the dynamics; the choice of a unit-trace target ensures that, at steady state ($\partial_t\mathbf{A}=0$), the solution coincides with a spatially smoothed version of $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$. Tracing \eqref{eq:A-strong-nd} and using $\mathrm{tr}\,\widehat{\mathbf{M}}=1$ gives
\begin{equation*}
\partial_t\big(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}\big) - \frac{\ell^2}{\tau_A}\,\Delta\big(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}\big) + \frac{1}{\tau_A}\,\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A} \;=\; \frac{1}{\tau_A},
\end{equation*}
so the spatial average of $\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}$ relaxes monotonically to $1$ under no-flux boundaries; pointwise, the relaxation term anchors $\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}$ toward unity while diffusion communicates this constraint across $\Omega$. The coupling \eqref{eq:M-def-nd}--\eqref{eq:M-hat-nd} aligns principal directions of $\mathbf{A}$ with those of a local strain metric, as visualized in Figure \ref{fig:fabric_orientation}, consistent with strain-adaptation hypotheses in trabecular microstructures, while maintaining global smoothness and avoiding non-differentiable thresholds.

Unless a sensitivity study dictates otherwise, we recommend using a unified smoothness $\varepsilon$ (and gate steepness $k$) across \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}, \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}, \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}, and \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd} by setting $\varepsilon_B\!=\!\varepsilon_M\!=\!\varepsilon$ and $k_B\!=\!k_M\!=\!k$. With the Gram maps \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} and \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, both $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$ are \emph{unit-trace and SPD} for any $\varepsilon>0$; the same $\mathcal{C}^\infty$ regularization supplies smooth clamps and lazy-zone weights in the density kinetics without explicit cone projections or case distinctions.

\section*{Constitutive Properties and Stability Analysis}

\subsection*{Gait Cycle Integration and Stimulus Accumulation}
The daily mechanical stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ aggregates the loading history over a full gait cycle. Following the Carter--Beaupr\'e theory, we employ a stress-power weighting:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x) = N_{\mathrm{cyc}} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \left( \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{vm}}(x,t)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right)^m \, \mathrm{d}t \approx N_{\mathrm{cyc}} \frac{\sum_i w_i (\sigma_i/\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}})^m}{\sum_i w_i}.
\end{equation}
The exponent $m$ plays a critical role in filtering the mechanical signal. For $m=1$, the stimulus is simply proportional to the average stress. However, empirical bone remodeling laws typically suggest $m \in [2, 4]$ or even higher. As $m$ increases, the stimulus becomes increasingly dominated by peak loads, while low-magnitude "background" loads contribute negligibly.

The dynamic gait load (solid line) generates a much stronger stimulus than a constant load of equivalent average magnitude (dashed line), highlighting the importance of peak magnitudes in the remodeling drive.}

This captures the biological reality that dynamic, high-impact activities are more osteogenic than static loading.

\subsection*{Stability Analysis: Stress vs. Strain and SED Drivers}
To elucidate the stability characteristics of the coupled constitutive laws, we perform a zero-dimensional (0D) phase space analysis. We consider a representative volume element with uniform density $\rho$ and stimulus $S$, subjected to uniaxial loading. We compare two common mechanical drivers: the stress-based driver used in our 3D formulation ($\psi_\sigma$) and a strain-energy density (SED) or strain-based driver ($\psi_\varepsilon$).

\paragraph{Stress Driver (Neutral Stability).}
Our formulation uses a stress-based driver $\psi_\sigma \propto (\sigma/\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}})^m$. The stress is constitutively defined as $\sigma(\rho,\varepsilon) = E(\rho)\varepsilon$. Under \emph{force control}, the external load $\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}$ is fixed, so equilibrium requires $\sigma = \sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}$. The strain must therefore adjust as $\varepsilon(\rho) = \sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}/E(\rho)$. Substituting this back into the driver:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_\sigma(\rho) = \left( \frac{E(\rho)\varepsilon(\rho)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right)^m = \left( \frac{E(\rho)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}} \cdot \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}}{E(\rho)} \right)^m = \left( \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right)^m = \mathrm{const}.
\end{equation}
Since the driver is constant with respect to density ($\partial \psi_\sigma / \partial \rho = 0$), the system exhibits \textbf{neutral stability}: a perturbation in density does not alter the stimulus. If the system is outside the lazy zone, it will continue to remodel indefinitely until it hits a bound ($\rho_{\min}$ or $\rho_{\max}$) or enters the lazy zone. There is no intrinsic feedback mechanism to arrest the growth or resorption.

\paragraph{Strain Driver (Negative Feedback).}
Consider a driver based on local strain magnitude, $\psi_{\varepsilon} = \varepsilon / \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}$. Under force control ($\sigma = \sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}$), the strain is inversely proportional to stiffness:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_{\varepsilon}(\rho) = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}}{E(\rho)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}} \quad \implies \quad \frac{\partial \psi_{\varepsilon}}{\partial \rho} = -\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}} E'(\rho)}{E(\rho)^2 \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}} < 0.
\end{equation}
Since $E'(\rho) > 0$, an increase in density reduces strain, lowering the stimulus. This provides a strong negative feedback loop that stabilizes the remodeling process.

\paragraph{Strain Energy Density (SED) Driver.}
Similarly, a driver based on strain energy density $U = \frac{1}{2}\sigma\varepsilon$ scales with the square of the stress under force control:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_{\mathrm{SED}}(\rho) = \frac{U}{U_{\mathrm{ref}}} = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}^2}{2 E(\rho) U_{\mathrm{ref}}} \quad \implies \quad \frac{\partial \psi_{\mathrm{SED}}}{\partial \rho} = -\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{ext}}^2 E'(\rho)}{2 E(\rho)^2 U_{\mathrm{ref}}} < 0.
\end{equation}
Both the Strain and SED drivers share the same stability characteristic: under force control, they both depend inversely on stiffness ($E^{-1}$), creating intrinsic self-stabilization.

\paragraph{Implications for 3D Modeling.}
While the SED driver offers superior intrinsic stability, we employ the stress driver $\psi_\sigma$ to capture specific physiological responses where bone mass correlates with load magnitude rather than deformation. To compensate for the lack of intrinsic negative feedback in the stress driver, our model relies on:
1.  \textbf{Bounded Driver:} The linear thermodynamic driver explicitly bounds the density within $[\rho_{\min}, \rho_{\max}]$ and drives it towards these bounds based on the sign of the stimulus.
2.  \textbf{Global Coupling:} In a redundant 3D structure, local stiffening sheds load to surrounding softer material (stress shielding), effectively reintroducing a form of negative feedback at the structural level even if the local constitutive law is neutrally stable.

Figure \ref{fig:mechanostat_optimization} illustrates these dynamics. Panel (A) shows the phase space trajectories. Panel (B) maps the stability regions. Panel (E) explicitly compares the feedback loops: the "Stress Control" curve is flat (neutral), while a hypothetical "Strain Control" curve would have a negative slope (intrinsic stability). This analysis justifies the necessity of the bounded driver formulation.

\section*{Backward--Euler time discretization}
\label{sec:be-time}
Let $0=t^0<t^1<\dots<t^N=T$ be a partition with $\Delta t^n:=t^n-t^{n-1}>0$. For any time-dependent field $X$, write $X^n\approx X(\cdot,t^n)$. Mechanics is quasi-static (no time derivative); $S$, $\rho$, and $\mathbf{A}$ are advanced by fully implicit Backward--Euler steps. Boundary and initial conditions are enforced at each time level exactly as in \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}, \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}, and \eqref{eq:A-bc-ic-nd}.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$.}
Find $\mathbf{u}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-mech}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\exp(\mathbf{L}^{n})\right)=\mathbf{0}\ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}^{n}=\mathbf{0}\ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\exp(\mathbf{L}^{n})\right)\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \text{on }\Gamma_N,
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ given by the Zysset-Curnier model \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}.

\paragraph{Stimulus.} (We keep the reaction coefficient $\tau_S$ explicit to match the implementation constants.) We take $\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}:=1$ to match the homeostatic reference level in~\eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd}.
Given $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-stim}
\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n}\big(S^{n}-S^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\kappa_S\,\Delta S^{n}\;+\;\tau_S\,S^{n}
\;=\; r_S\Big(\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}\Big)\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the Neumann condition from \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd} and $S^{0}=S_0$.

\paragraph{Density.}
Let $\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})$ be as in \eqref{eq:Drho-nd}. Find $\rho^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-rho}
\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\big(\rho^{n}-\rho^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})\,\nabla \rho^{n}\big)
\;=\; k_\rho \left[ S_+^n (\rho_{\max} - \rho^n) + S_-^n (\rho_{\min} - \rho^n) \right]\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd} and $\rho^{0}=\rho_0$.

\paragraph{Direction/fabric.}
With $\mathbf{L}_M(\mathbf{u}^{n})=\ln(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n}))$, find $\mathbf{L}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-A}
\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\big(\mathbf{L}^{n}-\mathbf{L}^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\frac{c_A\,\ell^2}{\tau_A}\,\Delta \mathbf{L}^{n}\;+\;\frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}^{n}
\;=\; \frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\,\mathbf{L}_M(\mathbf{u}^{n})\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the componentwise no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:L-bc-ic-nd} and $\mathbf{L}^{0}=\ln(\mathbf{A}_0)$.

\paragraph{Finite element spaces.}
For displacements we use the conforming space
\[
V_h:=\{\,\mathbf{v}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^d:\ \mathbf{v}_h=\mathbf{0}\text{ on }\Gamma_D\,\},
\]
for scalar fields $Q_h\subset H^1(\Omega)$, and for second-order tensors
\[
T_h:=\{\,\mathbf{Q}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^{d\times d}\,\}.
\]
Symmetry of $\mathbf{A}$ is respected by taking the symmetric part before the Gram normalisation \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} when building $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$ (weak form).}
Find $\mathbf{u}_h^{n}\in V_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{v}_h\in V_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-mech-unified}
a_u(\mathbf{u}_h^{n},\mathbf{v}_h;\rho^{n},\mathbf{L}^{n}) \;=\; \ell_u(\mathbf{v}_h),
\end{equation}
with the bilinear and linear forms
\begin{align*}
a_u(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{v};\rho,\mathbf{L})
&:= \int_\Omega \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{w}, \rho, \exp(\mathbf{L})) : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v}) \, \mathrm{d}x, \\[2mm]
\ell_u(\mathbf{v}) &:= \langle \mathbf{t},\mathbf{v} \rangle_{\Gamma_N},
\end{align*}
where $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is the Zysset-Curnier stress tensor defined in \eqref{eq:zysset-curnier}.

\paragraph{Stimulus (Backward--Euler weak form).}
Given $\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $q_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-stim-unified}
\left(\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n} S_h^{n}, q_h\right) \;+\; \kappa_S\,(\nabla S_h^{n}, \nabla q_h) \;+\; \tau_S\,(S_h^{n}, q_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n} S_h^{n-1}, q_h\right) \;+\; \big(r_S(\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}), q_h\big).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Density (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})$ from \eqref{eq:Drho-nd}, find $\rho_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $p_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-rho-unified}
\begin{aligned}
\left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n}, p_h\right) \;+\; \big(\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})\,\nabla \rho_h^{n}, \nabla p_h\big)
\;+\; \big(k_\rho (S_+^n + S_-^n)\,\rho_h^{n}, p_h\big) \\
\;=\; \left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n-1}, p_h\right) \;+\; \big(k_\rho (S_+^n \rho_{\max} + S_-^n \rho_{\min}), p_h\big),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $S_+^n = \max(0, S^n)$ and $S_-^n = \max(0, -S^n)$.

\paragraph{Direction/fabric (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{L}_M(\mathbf{u}^{n})=\ln(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n}))$, find $\mathbf{L}_h^{n}\in T_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{Q}_h\in T_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-A-unified}
\left(\left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n} + \frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\right)\mathbf{L}_h^{n}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; \frac{c_A\,\ell^2}{\tau_A}\,(\nabla \mathbf{L}_h^{n}, \nabla \mathbf{Q}_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\mathbf{L}_h^{n-1}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; \frac{c_A}{\tau_A}\,(\mathbf{L}_M(\mathbf{u}^{n}), \mathbf{Q}_h).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remarks.}
(i) Dirichlet conditions for mechanics are imposed strongly in $V_h$; natural boundary conditions enter via $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\Gamma_N}$. (ii) The tensor unknown $\mathbf{L}_h^{n}$ is represented componentwise; symmetry is preserved at the continuous level and is respected numerically. The fabric tensor is recovered as $\mathbf{A}^n = \exp(\mathbf{L}_h^n)$.

\section*{Fixed-point solver at a given time level: block Gauss--Seidel with Anderson acceleration}
\label{sec:fp-aa}
We describe the nonlinear solver used to obtain $(\mathbf{u}^n,S^n,\rho^n,\mathbf{L}^n)$ at a fixed time level $t^n$ from the Backward--Euler subproblems \eqref{eq:be-mech}--\eqref{eq:be-A}. Let the remodeling state be
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{X} \;:=\; Q\times T\times Q,\qquad
\mathbf{x} \;=\; (\rho,\mathbf{L},S) \in \mathcal{X},
\end{equation*}
with the product inner product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product-norm}
\langle \mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle_{\mathcal{X}} \;=\;
\alpha_\rho\,(\rho,p) \;+\; \alpha_L\,(\mathbf{L},\mathbf{Q}) \;+\; \alpha_S\,(S,q),
\end{equation}
for $\mathbf{y}=(p,\mathbf{Q},q)$ and positive weights $\alpha_\bullet$. The mechanics field $\mathbf{u}$ is treated as a dependent variable $\mathbf{u}(\rho,\mathbf{L})$ determined by the equilibrium equations. The merit/stopping metric is a projected 2--norm on the flattened state $(\rho,\mathbf{L},S)$ with RMS-per-DOF block weights (i.e., $w_i=1/N_i$) that projects out any constrained DOFs.

\paragraph{Block map.}
We define the fixed-point iteration in terms of the mechanical drivers $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ (scalar stimulus driver) and $\mathbf{L}_M$ (log-fabric target). Given the state $(\rho, \mathbf{L})$, the mechanics solver $\mathsf{U}$ determines equilibrium $\mathbf{u} = \mathsf{U}(\rho, \mathbf{L})$, which in turn generates the drivers:
\[
\psi_{\mathrm{new}} = \psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\mathbf{u}, \rho, \mathbf{L}), \qquad \mathbf{L}_{M,\mathrm{new}} = \ln(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u})).
\]
The block Gauss--Seidel map $\mathcal{G}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}$ is then defined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:block-map}
\mathcal{G}(\rho,\mathbf{L},S)
:=
\Big(\ \underbrace{\mathsf{R}(S_{\mathrm{new}},\mathbf{L})}_{\text{density}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{L}(\mathbf{L}_{M,\mathrm{new}})}_{\text{fabric}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{S}(\psi_{\mathrm{new}})}_{\text{stim}}\ \Big),
\end{equation}
where $S_{\mathrm{new}}=\mathsf{S}(\psi_{\mathrm{new}})$ is the updated stimulus. This structure highlights that the solvers $\mathsf{S}$ and $\mathsf{L}$ depend on the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ only through the intermediate drivers. In the femur application, the mechanics solver $\mathsf{U}$ builds a Carter/Beaupr\'e-style daily stress stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ from phase-specific Von Mises stresses via
$
J_{\mathrm{cycle}}(x)=\sum w\,(\sigma_{\mathrm{vm}}(x)/\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}})^{m}\big/\sum w,\quad
\psi_{\mathrm{day}}(x)=N_{\mathrm{cyc}}\,J_{\mathrm{cycle}}(x),
$
with exponent $m$ and cycle count $N_{\mathrm{cyc}}=\texttt{gait\_cycles\_per\_day}$ taken from \texttt{Config}; $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ then enters the stimulus source term.

The fixed point at $t^n$ solves
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp-residual}
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}^n)\;:=\;\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^n)-\mathbf{x}^n \;=\; \mathbf{0}\quad\text{in }\mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block Gauss--Seidel iteration.}
Given the state $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}=(\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{L}^{(m)},S^{(m)})$, we first compute the equilibrium displacement $\mathbf{u} = \mathsf{U}(\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{L}^{(m)})$ and evaluate the drivers $\psi = \psi_{\mathrm{day}}(\mathbf{u}, \rho^{(m)}, \mathbf{L}^{(m)})$ and $\mathbf{L}_M = \ln(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}))$. Then we perform the updates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gs-sweep}
\begin{aligned}
S^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{S}\big(\psi\big),\\
\rho^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{R}\big(S^{(m+1)},\mathbf{L}^{(m)}\big),\\
\mathbf{L}^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{L}\big(\mathbf{L}_M\big).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The sweep \eqref{eq:gs-sweep} defines the raw update $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}:=\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^{(m)})$. No external scalar damping $\omega_m$ is used; instead, damping/backtracking are handled inside the accelerator (see below).

\paragraph{Anderson acceleration (Type~II with $\sum\alpha=1$).}
We use a global, MPI-aware Type~II Anderson (Pulay) accelerator built on \emph{residual} snapshots
\(
r^{(j)}:=x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(j)}-\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}
\)
and corresponding states $\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}$. With window length $p$ (capped by $m$), form the residual stack $R=[r^{(m-p+1)},\dots,r^{(m)}]^\top$ and the \emph{global} Gram matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-gram}
H \;:=\; R\,R^\top \quad(\text{reduced across MPI ranks}),
\end{equation}
then solve the equality--constrained, Tikhonov-regularized system for the weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in\mathbb{R}^p$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kkt}
\begin{bmatrix} H + \lambda_{\mathrm{AA}} I & \mathbf{1} \\ \mathbf{1}^\top & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\alpha} \\ \eta \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{0} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\qquad
\lambda_{\mathrm{AA}} \;=\; \lambda\,\max\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{tr}(H)}{p},\,1\right).
\end{equation}
The accelerated candidate is then assembled as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-update}
\mathbf{y} \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \alpha_j\Big(\mathbf{x}^{(m-p+j)} + \beta\,r^{(m-p+j+1)}\Big),\qquad
\mathbf{s}:=\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)},
\end{equation}
with a \emph{step limiter} enforcing $\|\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}} - x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}} \le \mathrm{factor}\,\|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)} - \mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}$. The mixed iterate is $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}+\mathbf{s}$.

\paragraph{Projected-residual merit, safeguard, and backtracking.}
The acceptance test uses a \emph{projected, RMS-per-DOF block-weighted} 2--norm on the flattened state $(\rho,\mathbf{L},S)$: let $\mathsf{P}$ project out any constrained DOFs and choose weights $w_i=1/N_i$ for each block. Denote by $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{proj}}$ the resulting norm. With
\(
r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)} := \|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}
\)
and the \emph{proxy} for a candidate $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ given by
\(
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} := \|\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}-x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}},
\)
we accept the Anderson step iff
\begin{equation}\label{eq:safeguard}
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} \;\le\; (1-\gamma)\,r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}.
\end{equation}
Otherwise we backtrack along $\mathbf{s}$ by halving ($\le$ a user cap) until \eqref{eq:safeguard} holds; if backtracking fails, we \emph{fall back} to the raw Picard/GS iterate $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$. This safeguard is \emph{cheap}: it requires no extra GS sweep because the proxy only uses differences of already known states.

\paragraph{Automatic restarts and conditioning.}
The accelerator maintains the best Picard merit seen so far and schedules a history reset (on the \emph{next} call) if any of the following triggers fire: (i) a streak of $\ge k$ consecutive AA rejections; (ii) Picard merit stalls, i.e., $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}$ exceeds the best so far by a user factor; (iii) the estimated condition number of $H+\lambda_{\mathrm{AA}}I$ exceeds a threshold. In case of linear-algebra failure in \eqref{eq:kkt}, uniform weights are used as a safe fallback. No preconditioner-driven resets are used; preconditioner maintenance (if any) is decoupled from Anderson.

\paragraph{Stopping and logging.}
In the implementation we monitor the projected Picard residual $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}$ itself and stop once $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}<\mathrm{tol}$ and at least a user-specified minimum number of subiterations has been taken. The safeguard/backtracking \eqref{eq:safeguard} and step limiting ensure that any accepted Anderson step has a residual no worse than the contemporaneous Picard update in this metric. A separate \emph{blockwise relative iterate change} and Anderson diagnostics (history depth, conditioning, restarts) are recorded for analysis but are not used for stopping.

\paragraph{Well-posedness of subsolves.}
At each evaluation of $\mathcal{G}$ the four subproblems are uniformly elliptic (for fixed arguments) and admit unique solutions in the chosen finite-dimensional spaces: (i) mechanics has a coercive bilinear form by the density floor and $\nu\in(-1,\tfrac12)$, (ii) stimulus is SPD by $(c_S/\Delta t^n+\tau_S)$ with $\kappa_S\ge0$, (iii) density is SPD by $(1/\Delta t^n)+\lambda_\rho f(|S|_\varepsilon)\ge 1/\Delta t^n$ together with the elliptic floor in $\mathbf{D}_\rho$, and (iv) fabric is SPD by $(c_A/\Delta t^n)+\tau_A>0$ and $c_A\ell^2>0$. Thus each application of $\mathcal{G}$ is well-defined.

\paragraph{Convergence discussion.}
On bounded sets where the smooth gates/clamps are Lipschitz, $\mathcal{G}$ is locally Lipschitz. If the induced modulus $L<1$, the undamped Gauss--Seidel iteration is linearly convergent. The safeguard \eqref{eq:safeguard} enforces a strict improvement over the contemporaneous Picard residual (by factor $1-\gamma$); combined with step limiting and automatic restarts, this yields robust global behavior of the AA-wrapped method even when the least-squares system \eqref{eq:kkt} is poorly conditioned.

\paragraph{Algorithm (per time level $t^n$).}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Initialize $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}$ (from $t^{n-1}$ or a warm start) and the AA history; set counters/timers to zero.
\item Perform one GS sweep to obtain $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$ and set $\mathbf{x}^{(1)}\!\leftarrow\!x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$.
\item For $m=1,2,\dots$ until the stopping test holds:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform one GS sweep to get $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$.
  \item If AA is enabled, form $H$ from residual histories, solve \eqref{eq:kkt}, assemble $\mathbf{y}$, apply step limiting, and build $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$.
  \item Evaluate the projected-residual merit and apply the safeguard/backtracking \eqref{eq:safeguard}; fall back to Picard if needed.
  \item Accept the step, update histories (and schedule a restart if a trigger fires), and log diagnostics.
  \end{enumerate}
\item Output $\mathbf{x}^{n}:=\mathbf{x}^{(m_\star)}$ for the first index $m_\star$ meeting the stopping criterion.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Physical Parameter Analysis and Model Calibration}

We adopt a consistent set of physical parameters for both the full-scale femur simulations and the representative volume element (RVE) verification tests. The reference implementation uses a unit system of millimeters [mm], days [day], tonnes [t], and megapascals [MPa].

\paragraph{Elasticity and Anisotropy.}
The bone matrix is modeled using the Zysset-Curnier orthotropic formulation. The stiffness tensors are constructed from the eigenvalues of the fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$. The material parameters are: axial modulus $E_0=15\,\mathrm{GPa}$, shear modulus $G_0=5\,\mathrm{GPa}$, and Poisson's ratio $\nu_0=0.3$. The density exponent is $k=1.9$ and the fabric exponent is $p=1.0$.

\paragraph{Mechanoregulation and Stimulus.}
The remodeling signal is driven by the deviation of the daily stress stimulus $\psi_{\mathrm{day}}$ from a homeostatic reference $\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}=20.0\,\mathrm{MPa}$. The stimulus field $S$ evolves via a diffusion-reaction equation with capacity $c_S=1.0$, intrinsic decay $\tau_S=1.0\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}$, and diffusivity $\kappa_S=1.0\,\mathrm{mm}^2\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}$. The transduction gain is set to $r_S=1.0\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}$.

\paragraph{Density Evolution.}
Density kinetics follow a linear thermodynamic driver. The rate constant is set to $k_\rho = 0.1\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}$. The density is bounded by $\rho_{\min}=0.05\,\mathrm{g/cm}^3$ and $\rho_{\max}=1.8\,\mathrm{g/cm}^3$. Spatial regularization is provided by anisotropic density diffusion with $\beta_{\parallel}=\beta_{\perp}=0.1\,\mathrm{mm}^2\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}$.

\paragraph{Fabric Evolution.}
The fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$ relaxes toward the loading-induced target $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$ with a time constant $\tau_A=100.0\,\mathrm{days}$ and capacity $c_A=1.0$. Spatial smoothness is enforced by a diffusion length scale $\ell=2.0\,\mathrm{mm}$, preventing the formation of singular orientation fields.

\paragraph{Calibration for Convergence Studies.}
For the spatial convergence analysis presented in \ref{app:convergence}, we employ a cubic control volume of side length $L=5\,\mathrm{mm}$. The material parameters match the baseline set above. Loading is applied via Periodic (PMUBC) or Kinematic (KUBC) boundary conditions to generate an apparent strain of magnitude $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}=10^{-3}$, ensuring the response remains within the linear elastic regime.

\subsection*{Linear solver defaults and preconditioner maintenance}\label{subsec:ksp_defaults}
Unless stated otherwise, each linear block solve uses PETSc MINRES with GAMG preconditioning. In the implementation, each subsolver keeps a single PETSc KSP object that is reused as $(\rho,\mathbf{A},S)$ evolve, with updated operators supplied via \texttt{setOperators}; preconditioner setup is therefore decoupled from the AA/GS logic and follows PETSc's standard reuse policy.
