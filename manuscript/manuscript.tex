\begin{abstract}
We propose a four-field bone-remodeling formulation that co-evolves displacement $\mathbf{u}$, apparent density $\rho$, fabric $\mathbf{A}$, and a nonlocal mechanotransductive stimulus $S$. The elastic response couples a density-scaled isotropic baseline with a nonnegative rank-one reinforcement aligned to a trace-safe normalization of $\mathbf{A}$ (without explicit PSD projection). The stimulus follows a diffusion--reaction balance driven by elastic energy; density evolves via anisotropic transport guided by $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ and a smooth stimulus-gated relaxation to $[\rho_{\min},\rho_{\max}]$; fabric relaxes toward a trace-safe normalization of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ with spatial diffusion. Time integration is fully implicit Backward--Euler; space is discretized by conforming $H^1$ finite elements in a unified weak-form notation. Nonlinear coupling is solved by a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel sweep (mechanics $\rightarrow$ stimulus $\rightarrow$ density $\rightarrow$ fabric) wrapped in Type-I Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and residual safeguards. A single smoothing policy used across clamping, stimulus splits, and normalizations ensures differentiability and preserves coercivity, while the solver architecture yields stable, mesh-robust convergence without monolithic Jacobians.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
\label{s:introduction}
Bone is a living composite that continually reorganizes its microstructure to balance mechanical function with metabolic cost. Four mechanobiologically distinct processes drive this adaptation: (i) the elastic response of the solid matrix to loads, (ii) the evolution of mass (apparent density/porosity), (iii) the reorientation of the trabecular/collagen network (fabric anisotropy), and (iv) the production and spatial transport of cell-mediated biochemical signals that couple local mechanics to osteoblast/osteoclast activity. Robust numerical prediction of morphology and stiffness therefore requires a model that co-evolves these \emph{four} fields---displacement $u$, density $\rho$, fabric tensor $\mathbf{A}$, and a diffusive mechanotransductive stimulus $S$---within a single, tightly coupled formulation.

Mechanobiology supports this coupling. First, the mechanical field is not merely a passive input: Turner’s “three rules” assert that the magnitude, rate (frequency), and rest intervals of \emph{dynamic} loading govern the osteogenic response \cite{Turner1998_ThreeRulesBone}. Spatial \emph{strain gradients}---proxies for lacuno--canalicular (LCN) fluid flow---localize bone formation more reliably than strain magnitude alone, as shown in turkey radius models \cite{Gross1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate} and in exercise-induced periosteal activation in rooster models \cite{Zernicke1997_StrainGradientsCorrelate}. These gradients act through osteocytes embedded in the LCN, where fluid shear induces Ca$^{2+}$/Wnt signaling; acute loading suppresses sclerostin (Sost), while unloading elevates it, shifting the local formation/resorption balance \cite{Robling2008_MechanicalStimulationBone}. While mechanosensitive channels such as PIEZO1 contribute to skeletal homeostasis, their cell-type-specific roles in load adaptation are nuanced and do not obviate the need to resolve the full biophysical cascade from matrix strains to fluid shear to osteocytic signaling \cite{Wang2020_MechanicalSensingProtein}. Together, these findings motivate an explicit stimulus field $S$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical cues and \emph{transported} over osteocytic/vascular pathways (e.g., via diffusion--decay dynamics), rather than a purely local, algebraic “mechanical index.”

Second, accurate mechanics--morphology feedback requires density $\rho$ and anisotropy $\mathbf{A}$ to co-evolve and feed back into the elastic moduli. Empirically, bone volume fraction (BV/TV) explains most variance in cancellous stiffness, and fabric anisotropy accounts for nearly all of the remainder; together they reach $\sim$95--98\% explanatory power across anatomical sites, with negligible contribution from other morphometric covariates \cite{Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Theoretical and constitutive links between $\{\rho,\mathbf{A}\}$ and orthotropic elastic constants are well established---Cowin’s fabric--elasticity relations (1985, 1990) and the Zysset--Curnier orthotropic model (1995) connect BV/TV and second-order fabric tensors to the apparent stiffness tensor (including $E$, $G$, and $\nu$) \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic}. In practice, fabric can be quantified directly from imaging using gray-level structure-tensor or mean-intercept-length methods and validated on HR-pQCT/$\mu$CT, enabling evolution laws for $\mathbf{A}$ aligned with principal stress/flow directions \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Ali2017_Mu, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}. As $\rho$ and $\mathbf{A}$ change through remodeling, the spatial stiffness field---and thus the displacement field $u$ and its gradients---must be updated consistently to avoid spurious transients and mesh-dependent artifacts; morphology--elasticity models with full fabric information achieve the highest predictive accuracy in such coupled simulations \cite{Schneider2012_MorphologyElasticityRelationships, Panyasantis2016_EffectiveElasticProperties}.

Third, the remodeling stimulus that drives BMU-scale biology is not localized to a voxel or finite element. Experiments and theory both indicate spatial nonlocality through osteocytic/vascular networks; modern formulations therefore promote the stimulus to a \emph{field} obeying a diffusion--reaction equation, which systematically removes unphysical checkerboarding and captures healing or damage--remodeling interactions \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. Recent work also explores multi-stimulus energetics (e.g., shear vs.\ normal components) in continuum settings, reinforcing the need to track multiple evolving material parameters beyond a single density variable \cite{Branecka2024_BoneRemodelingModel}.

From a numerical standpoint, loosely coupled or explicit updates are in general more fragile than monolithic schemes: density-only algorithms are prone to mesh-dependent checkerboards and near-field oscillations near load singularities \cite{Jacobs1995_NumericalInstabilitiesBone, Kobler2024_GradientEnhancedBone}. We therefore retain a partitioned block Gauss--Seidel fixed point and wrap it in Anderson acceleration with Tikhonov regularization and safeguards; see the solver description in \S\ref{sec:fp-aa}. This combination substantially mitigates splitting artifacts and yields robust, quasi mesh-independent convergence in our experiments \cite{Both2019_AndersonAcceleratedFixed, Walker2011_AndersonAcceleration, Fang2009_TwoClassesMultisecant} while remaining minimally invasive on top of standard FE components.

\section*{Comparison with related models and novelty}
\label{sec:related-novelty}
Our formulation sits between classical density--only mechanostat models and fully coupled cell--population or damage--remodeling frameworks. In \emph{local} mechanostat models the remodeling drive is a pointwise function of a mechanical index, typically strain or strain--energy density, and only the density $\rho$ evolves; transport and fabric are absent and spatial regularity is supplied ad hoc (cf.\ early density models and follow--ups reviewed in \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological}). Nonlocal and \emph{diffusive} stimulus fields---as in \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd}---have been advocated to account for osteocytic/vascular communication and to remove checkerboarding; this strand has gained traction in the last decade \cite{Giorgio2019_MechanicallyDrivenBiological, Addessi2024_BoneRemodelingApproach}. On the elasticity side, orthotropic models that couple BV/TV and second--order fabric tensors to the full fourth--order stiffness tensor (Cowin's relations and the Zysset--Curnier model) are now standard in homogenized micro--FE and hFE pipelines \cite{Cowin1985_RelationshipBetweenElasticity, Turner1990_FabricDependenceOrthotropic, Zysset1995_AlternativeModelAnisotropic, Maquer2015_BoneVolumeFraction, Maquer2017_NotOnlyStiffness}. Our constitutive choice preserves this spirit but adopts an energetically nonnegative, rank--one reinforcement aligned with a unit--trace direction proxy $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$, which empirically captures the dominant directional effect while simplifying the numerics; it leaves the isotropic floor strictly coercive through the smooth clamp $\widetilde\rho\ge\rho_{\min}$, cf.\ \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}.

In the \emph{evolution} of anisotropy, several authors align fabric with stress or strain measures and regularize it by diffusion or smoothing (e.g.\ structure--tensor approaches and stress--alignment hypotheses, see \cite{Kuo2007_QuantifyingAnisotropyTrabecular, Rietbergen2015_NovelApproachEstimate, Dutra2016_QuantifyingTrabecularBone}). We follow this line by relaxing $\mathbf{A}$ toward the trace--safe normalization $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$ of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, which robustly aligns principal directions while avoiding non--differentiable projections and without enforcing positive semidefiniteness of $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ (the isotropic fallback and transport floor suffice in practice).

\section*{Strong form (nondimensionalized) equations}\label{sec:strong-forms-nd}
\paragraph{Scaling and nondimensionalization.}
We adopt characteristic scales $L_c$ (length), $u_c$ (displacement), $\rho_c$ (apparent density), $E_0$ (reference modulus), and $t_c=1/\tau_S^{\rm dim}$ (time). Stresses and energy are measured by $\sigma_c=E_0(u_c/L_c)$ and $\psi_c=E_0(u_c/L_c)^2$. Dimensional fields $(x,t,\mathbf{u},\rho,S,\mathbf{A})$ map to dimensionless ones via
\[
x=L_c\,x',\quad t=t_c\,t',\quad \mathbf{u}=u_c\,\mathbf{u}',\quad
\rho=\rho_c\,\rho',\quad S=\frac{1}{\gamma_\rho t_c}\,S',\quad \mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}'.
\]
All equations below are written in primeless variables after dropping the primes. The boundary partition is $\partial\Omega=\Gamma_D\cup\Gamma_N$ with outward unit normal $\mathbf{n}$. The nondimensional traction vector $\mathbf{t}$ is scaled by $\sigma_c$; for example, uniaxial compression is expressed as $\mathbf{t}=(-t_p/\sigma_c,0,\ldots)$ on $\Gamma_N$. We use a \emph{single} smoothness parameter $\varepsilon>0$ consistently across operators whenever possible (density clamp, stimulus split, and trace-safe normalizations); when separate symbols appear (e.g., $\varepsilon_B$ in the direction target), they default to $\varepsilon$ unless stated otherwise for numerical reasons. Likewise, we keep a single steepness parameter $k>0$ for gating functions, with $k_B=k$ unless noted.

\subsection*{Mechanical submodel}
\label{subsec:mech-nd}
\paragraph{Kinematics and equilibrium.}
We adopt small-strain kinematics with the symmetric gradient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-eps-nd}
\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}) := \tfrac12\big(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^\top\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}_{\rm sym},\qquad d\in\{2,3\}.
\end{equation}
Under quasi-static conditions the balance of linear momentum reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech-strong-nd}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \ \text{on }\Gamma_N.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Constitutive structure.}
The stress response consists of an isotropic baseline whose stiffness scales with apparent density and a directional reinforcement aligned with the local fabric. Let $\nu\in(-1,1/2)$ be Poisson’s ratio and $n>0$ an exponent. The density--modulus relation is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E-rho-nd}
\begin{aligned}
E(\rho) & = E_0\,\widetilde{\rho}^{\,n},\\
\lambda(\rho) & = \frac{E(\rho)\,\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)},\\
\mu(\rho) & = \frac{E(\rho)}{2(1+\nu)}.
\end{aligned}\end{equation}
The stress law is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stress-law-nd}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})
= 2\,\mu(\rho)\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})
\;+\; \lambda(\rho)\,\mathrm{tr}\!\big(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\big)\,\mathbf{I}
\;+\; \xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\,E(\rho)\,\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}) \big\rangle\,\widehat{\mathbf{A}}.
\end{equation}
Here $\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\ge 0$ scales the anisotropic reinforcement, $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ denotes the Frobenius inner product, and $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ is a symmetric, unit-trace proxy of $\mathbf{A}$ constructed below. The last term is the action of the rank-one fourth-order operator $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}\!\otimes\!\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ on $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})$ since
\[
(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}\!\otimes\!\widehat{\mathbf{A}}):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}
=\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\big\rangle\,\widehat{\mathbf{A}},
\qquad
\forall\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}_{\rm sym}.
\]
The associated quadratic contribution is nonnegative:
\[
\big(\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\big\rangle\,\widehat{\mathbf{A}}\big):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}
=\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\big\rangle^2\ge 0.
\]

\paragraph{Smooth lower density clamp.}
To maintain uniform strong ellipticity and avoid case distinctions in highly porous regions, we use the same smooth ``max'' as in the implementation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smooth-max-nd}
\widetilde{\rho}(\rho;\rho_{\min},\varepsilon)
:=\rho_{\min}+\tfrac12\!\left[(\rho-\rho_{\min})+\sqrt{(\rho-\rho_{\min})^2+\varepsilon^2}\right].
\end{equation}
This monotone, everywhere differentiable approximation of $\max\{\rho,\rho_{\min}\}$ enforces $\widetilde{\rho}\ge\rho_{\min}$ with a single global smoothing width $\varepsilon>0$. No upper clamp is imposed.

\paragraph{Trace-safe smooth normalization of the direction field.}
We form the symmetric part $\mathbf{A}_{\mathrm{sym}}:=\tfrac12(\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{A}^\top)$ and normalize it to a unit-trace tensor using a smooth Gram construction:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}
\widehat{\mathbf{A}} \;:=\; \frac{\,\mathbf{A}_{\mathrm{sym}}^\top \mathbf{A}_{\mathrm{sym}} + \varepsilon\,\mathbf{I}\,}{\mathrm{tr}\!\left(\mathbf{A}_{\mathrm{sym}}^\top \mathbf{A}_{\mathrm{sym}} + \varepsilon\,\mathbf{I}\right)}.
\end{equation}
This map yields a \emph{symmetric, unit-trace} tensor; for any $\varepsilon>0$ it is in fact \emph{positive-definite}. No conditional branching or explicit cone projections are used.

\paragraph{Ellipticity and energy bounds without degeneracy.}
Define the nondimensional strain-energy density
\begin{equation*}
\psi(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})
:=\mu(\rho)\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})
+\tfrac12\,\lambda(\rho)\,\big(\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\big)^2
+\tfrac12\,\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\,E(\rho)\,\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\big\rangle^2 .
\end{equation*}
Using \eqref{eq:E-rho-nd}--\eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} with $\rho_{\min}>0$ and $\nu\in(-1,1/2)$, and applying Korn’s inequality on $\Omega$, there exists $c_0>0$ depending only on $(\nu,n,\rho_{\min},E_0)$ and the domain such that
\begin{equation*}
\int_\Omega \psi(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathrm{d}x \ \ge\ c_0\,\|\mathbf{u}\|_{H^1(\Omega)}^2.
\end{equation*}
The anisotropic contribution is nonnegative because it is quadratic in $\langle\widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\rangle$. Although the anisotropic reinforcement in \eqref{eq:stress-law-nd} is introduced at the constitutive level rather than derived from a fully coupled energy for $(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{A})$, at each mechanics step with $(\rho,\widehat{\mathbf{A}})$ held fixed it contributes the quadratic term
\begin{equation*}
\tfrac12\,\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\,E(\rho)\,\big\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\big\rangle^2 \;\ge\; 0.
\end{equation*}
Hence it cannot deteriorate the Legendre--Hadamard constant of the isotropic baseline; the strong ellipticity inherited from $\mu(\rho)>0$ and $\lambda(\rho)+\tfrac{2}{d}\mu(\rho)>0$ (uniformly ensured by the smooth lower clamp $\widetilde{\rho}\ge\rho_{\min}>0$) is preserved and may only be improved along the principal directions selected by $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$. The strain-energy density therefore satisfies the pointwise lower bound
\begin{equation*}
\psi(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon};\rho,\widehat{\mathbf{A}})\ \ge\ c_{\mathrm{iso}}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!:\!\boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
\end{equation*}
with a constant $c_{\mathrm{iso}}>0$ depending only on $(\nu,n,\rho_{\min},E_0)$. Korn’s inequality then yields the standard global coercivity.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
The density--modulus law \eqref{eq:E-rho-nd} encodes the empirical power-law scaling between bone volume fraction (BV/TV) and apparent stiffness. The directional reinforcement supplements this isotropic baseline by increasing stiffness along preferred fabric directions, consistent with homogenized tissue-level elasticity models and with observations of strain-adaptive anisotropy in trabecular bone. The smooth lower clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd} guarantees strictly positive stiffness even in highly porous regions, thus preventing mechanical degeneracy and preserving differentiability. The normalization \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} filters the computed direction field into a \emph{symmetric, unit-trace} descriptor; with $\varepsilon>0$ it is \emph{positive-definite}. This combination yields a mechanically transparent, numerically robust model suitable for coupling with remodeling stimuli and evolution equations.

\subsection*{Stimulus submodel}\label{subsec:stim-nd}
We represent the mechanotransductive drive by a dimensionless scalar field $S:\Omega\times(0,T]\to\mathbb{R}$ that is \emph{generated} by mechanical activity and \emph{transported} through osteocytic/vascular pathways. Positive values correspond to net formation drive and negative values to net resorption drive; downstream remodeling laws act on smooth decompositions $S^\pm$ and $|S|$, so the sign of $S$ is intentional and no positivity constraint is imposed on the stimulus itself.

The stimulus obeys a linear diffusion--reaction balance with a source proportional to departures from a homeostatic elastic-energy level,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-strong-nd}
c_S\,\partial_t S \;-\; \kappa_S\,\Delta S \;+\; \tau_S\,S
\;=\; r_S\big(\psi(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}\big)
\quad\text{in }\Omega\times(0,T],
\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous Neumann boundary conditions and an initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}
\nabla S\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\ \ \text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
S(\cdot,0)=S_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $c_S>0$ denotes a temporal capacity, $\kappa_S\ge 0$ a diffusivity, and $\tau_S>0$ the reaction/turnover rate. (Choosing $t_c=1/\tau_S^{\rm dim}$ would set $\tau_S\equiv1$ in nondimensional variables, but in the implementation we \emph{retain} $\tau_S$ explicitly.) The mechanical drive is the instantaneous nondimensional elastic-energy density
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stim-psi-nd}
\psi(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A}) \;=\; \tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A}):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\ \ge 0,
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ defined in the mechanical model.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:stim-strong-nd} acts as a spatio-temporal low-pass filter of the mechanical drive $\psi-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}$. Regions of sustained supra-homeostatic energy ($\psi>\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}$) accumulate positive stimulus, while sub-homeostatic regions accumulate negative stimulus. Diffusion broadens and smooths these regions over the characteristic length
\(
\ell_S=\sqrt{\kappa_S/\tau_S}
\)
(in general), while the reaction term damps transient fluctuations with a characteristic time
\(
t_S=c_S/\tau_S
\).
The capacity $c_S$ thus sets the inertia with respect to temporal fluctuations in the drive, $\kappa_S$ controls spatial spread via gap-junction/vascular pathways, and $\tau_S$ sets the local turnover toward homeostasis. The homogeneous Neumann condition corresponds to zero normal flux of $S$ at $\partial\Omega$.

\subsection*{Density submodel}\label{subsec:density-nd}
The nondimensional apparent density $\rho=\rho(x,t)$ evolves under anisotropic transport aligned with the fabric and under a stimulus-driven relaxation toward admissible bounds $\rho_{\min}$ and $\rho_{\max}$ (both nondimensional). The stimulus $S=S(x,t)$ is the dimensionless field introduced in the stimulus submodel; its sign distinguishes formation ($S>0$) from resorption ($S<0$). The nondimensional strong form reads, for $(x,t)\in\Omega\times(0,T]$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-strong-nd}
\begin{aligned}
\partial_t \rho \;&-\; \nabla\!\cdot\!\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho(x,t)\,\nabla\rho\big)
\;+\; \big|S\big|_{\varepsilon}\,\rho
\;=\; S_{\varepsilon}^{+}\,\rho_{\max} + S_{\varepsilon}^{-}\,\rho_{\min}.
\end{aligned}\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}
\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho\,\nabla\rho\big)\!\cdot\!\mathbf{n}=0\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],\qquad
\rho(\cdot,0)=\rho_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega,\ \ \rho_{\min}\le \rho_0\le \rho_{\max}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Anisotropic transport.}
Directional transport is modeled by the symmetric diffusion tensor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Drho-nd}
\mathbf{D}_\rho(x,t) \;=\; \beta_{\perp}\,\mathbf{I} \;+\; \big(\beta_{\parallel}-\beta_{\perp}\big)\,\widehat{\mathbf{A}}(x,t),
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{\parallel},\beta_{\perp}>0$ are nondimensional diffusivities along and transverse to the fabric, respectively, and $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}(x,t)$ is the \emph{unit-trace, symmetric, positive-definite} fabric tensor obtained by the Gram normalisation described above. With $\beta_{\parallel},\beta_{\perp}>0$ this yields a uniformly positive-definite $\mathbf{D}_\rho$ without any conditional branching or explicit cone projections.

\paragraph{Stimulus split and kinetic rate.}
To keep the right-hand side and the damping term in \eqref{eq:density-strong-nd} smooth at $S=0$, we adopt a single $\mathcal{C}^{\infty}$ approximation of the absolute value and use it to define the positive/negative parts:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smooth-splits-nd}
|S|_{\varepsilon}:=\sqrt{S^2+\varepsilon^2},\qquad
S_{\varepsilon}^{\pm} := \tfrac12\!\left(|S|_{\varepsilon} \pm S\right).
\end{equation}
For $|S|\gg \varepsilon$ this coincides with the exact split; in a small neighborhood of the origin it provides a simple, globally smooth transition used consistently across the scheme.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:density-strong-nd} combines anisotropic diffusion with a stimulus-gated logistic relaxation. The diffusion term $\nabla\!\cdot(\mathbf{D}_\rho\nabla\rho)$ spreads mass preferentially along the principal directions of $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$, while the reaction terms on the right- and left-hand sides drive $\rho$ toward $\rho_{\max}$ where $S>0$ and toward $\rho_{\min}$ where $S<0$. The damping $|S|_{\varepsilon}\,\rho$ guarantees that, in regions of large $|S|$, the relaxation is fast and saturates at the appropriate bound, whereas in regions where $S\approx 0$ the dynamics is transport-dominated. Under the no-flux boundary condition \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd} the admissible interval $[\rho_{\min},\rho_{\max}]$ is invariant in the sense of the parabolic comparison principle: if $\rho_{\min}\le \rho_0\le \rho_{\max}$ a.e.\ in $\Omega$, then $\rho_{\min}\le \rho(\cdot,t)\le \rho_{\max}$ for all $t\in(0,T]$. Physically, \eqref{eq:density-strong-nd} states that sustained supra-homeostatic stimulus accumulates mass up to $\rho_{\max}$, sustained sub-homeostatic stimulus removes mass down to $\rho_{\min}$, and the anisotropic transport couples neighboring regions along the evolving fabric architecture.

\subsection*{Direction (fabric) submodel}\label{subsec:direction-nd}
The local fabric is described by a second-order, dimensionless tensor field $\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}(x,t)\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ ($d\in\{2,3\}$), which aligns with mechanically preferred directions and is smoothed in space by diffusion. We regard $\mathbf{A}$ as symmetric and treat its dynamics as a relaxation--diffusion flow toward a unit-trace, positive-definite target extracted from the current mechanical state. The nondimensional strong form on a bounded Lipschitz domain is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A-strong-nd}
\begin{aligned}
c_A\,\partial_t \mathbf{A} \;-\; c_A\,\ell^2\,\Delta \mathbf{A} \;+\; \tau_A\,\mathbf{A}
\;=\; \tau_A\,\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}) \,.
\end{aligned}\end{equation}
supplemented with homogeneous no-flux boundary conditions and an admissible initial state,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A-bc-ic-nd}
\partial_{\mathbf{n}}\mathbf{A}:=\big(\nabla \mathbf{A}\big)\,\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{0}\quad\text{on }\partial\Omega\times(0,T],
\qquad
\mathbf{A}(\cdot,0)=\mathbf{A}_0\ \ \text{in }\Omega.
\end{equation}
Here $\ell>0$ is a nondimensional interaction length, $c_A>0$ a capacity, and $\tau_A>0$ a relaxation rate. The Laplacian and normal derivative act componentwise on $\mathbf{A}$. Equation \eqref{eq:A-strong-nd} is linear and uniformly parabolic in space--time for $\ell>0$ (and uniformly elliptic in space at each time with $\tau_A>0$).

\paragraph{Target construction from mechanics.}
Let $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})$ be the small-strain tensor from the mechanical submodel. The instantaneous, dimensionless alignment tensor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-def-nd}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{u}) \;:=\; \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})^\top\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})
\end{equation}
is symmetric and positive semidefinite. We normalize it by the same \emph{trace-safe shift-and-rescale} used throughout (no blending or gating),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-hat-nd}
\widehat{\mathbf{M}} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{M}+\varepsilon_M\,\mathbf{I}}{\mathrm{tr}\!\big(\mathbf{M}+\varepsilon_M\,\mathbf{I}\big)}\,,
\end{equation}
which is smooth in $\mathbf{M}$, symmetric, \emph{positive-definite for $\varepsilon_M>0$}, and unit-trace by construction.

\paragraph{Quantitative analysis and biological interpretation.}
The reaction term $\tau_A(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}-\mathbf{A})$ in \eqref{eq:A-strong-nd} drives $\mathbf{A}$ toward the mechanically informed, unit-trace target, while the diffusion $-\ell^2 c_A\Delta\mathbf{A}$ spreads directional information and regularizes spatial oscillations. The homogeneous Neumann boundary condition represents no flux of directional content across $\partial\Omega$. Symmetry is preserved by the dynamics; the choice of a unit-trace target ensures that, at steady state ($\partial_t\mathbf{A}=0$), the solution coincides with a spatially smoothed version of $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$. Tracing \eqref{eq:A-strong-nd} and using $\mathrm{tr}\,\widehat{\mathbf{M}}=1$ gives
\begin{equation*}
\partial_t\big(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}\big) - \ell^2\,\Delta\big(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}\big) + \frac{\tau_A}{c_A}\,\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A} \;=\; \frac{\tau_A}{c_A},
\end{equation*}
so the spatial average of $\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}$ relaxes monotonically to $1$ under no-flux boundaries; pointwise, the relaxation term anchors $\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{A}$ toward unity while diffusion communicates this constraint across $\Omega$. The coupling \eqref{eq:M-def-nd}--\eqref{eq:M-hat-nd} aligns principal directions of $\mathbf{A}$ with those of a local strain metric, consistent with strain-adaptation hypotheses in trabecular microstructures, while maintaining global smoothness and avoiding non-differentiable thresholds.

\paragraph{Regularization parameters and defaults.}
Unless a sensitivity study dictates otherwise, we recommend using a unified smoothness $\varepsilon$ (and gate steepness $k$) across \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}, \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}, \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}, and \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd} by setting $\varepsilon_B\!=\!\varepsilon_M\!=\!\varepsilon$ and $k_B\!=\!k_M\!=\!k$. With the Gram maps \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} and \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, both $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}$ are \emph{unit-trace and SPD} for any $\varepsilon>0$; no cone projections or conditional branches are required.

\section*{Backward--Euler time discretization (nondimensional)}
\label{sec:be-time}
Let $0=t^0<t^1<\dots<t^N=T$ be a partition with $\Delta t^n:=t^n-t^{n-1}>0$. For any time-dependent field $X$, write $X^n\approx X(\cdot,t^n)$. Mechanics is quasi-static (no time derivative); $S$, $\rho$, and $\mathbf{A}$ are advanced by fully implicit Backward--Euler steps. Boundary and initial conditions are enforced at each time level exactly as in \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd}, \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd}, and \eqref{eq:A-bc-ic-nd}.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$.}
Find $\mathbf{u}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-mech}
-\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n}\right)=\mathbf{0}\ \text{in }\Omega,\qquad
\mathbf{u}^{n}=\mathbf{0}\ \text{on }\Gamma_D,\qquad
\boldsymbol{\sigma}\!\left(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n}\right)\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{t}\ \text{on }\Gamma_N,
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ given by \eqref{eq:stress-law-nd}, $E(\rho)$ by \eqref{eq:E-rho-nd} with the lower clamp \eqref{eq:smooth-max-nd}, and $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ built from $\mathbf{A}^{n}$ via \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}.

\paragraph{Stimulus.} (We keep the reaction coefficient $\tau_S$ explicit to match the implementation constants.)
Given $\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-stim}
\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n}\big(S^{n}-S^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\kappa_S\,\Delta S^{n}\;+\;\tau_S\,S^{n}
\;=\; r_S\Big(\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}\Big)\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the Neumann condition from \eqref{eq:stim-bc-ic-nd} and $S^{0}=S_0$.

\paragraph{Density.}
Let $\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})$ be as in \eqref{eq:Drho-nd} and let $|S|_{\varepsilon}$ and $S^\pm_{\varepsilon}$ be as in \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}. Find $\rho^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-rho}
\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\big(\rho^{n}-\rho^{n-1}\big)\;-\;\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})\,\nabla \rho^{n}\big)\;+\; |S^{n}|_{\varepsilon}\,\rho^{n}
\;=\; S_{\varepsilon}^{n,+}\,\rho_{\max} \;+\; S_{\varepsilon}^{n,-}\,\rho_{\min}\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:density-bc-ic-nd} and $\rho^{0}=\rho_0$.

\paragraph{Direction/fabric.}
With $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{u}^{n})=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}^{n})^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}^{n})$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, find $\mathbf{A}^{n}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be-A}
\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\big(\mathbf{A}^{n}-\mathbf{A}^{n-1}\big)\;-\;c_A\,\ell^2\,\Delta \mathbf{A}^{n}\;+\;\tau_A\,\mathbf{A}^{n}
\;=\; \tau_A\,\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n})\quad\text{in }\Omega,
\end{equation}
with the componentwise no--flux condition from \eqref{eq:A-bc-ic-nd} and $\mathbf{A}^{0}=\mathbf{A}_0$.

\paragraph{Finite element spaces.}
For displacements we use the conforming space
\[
V_h:=\{\,\mathbf{v}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^d:\ \mathbf{v}_h=\mathbf{0}\text{ on }\Gamma_D\,\},
\]
for scalar fields $Q_h\subset H^1(\Omega)$, and for second-order tensors
\[
T_h:=\{\,\mathbf{Q}_h\in [H^1(\Omega)]^{d\times d}\,\}.
\]
Symmetry of $\mathbf{A}$ is respected by using $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\cdot)$ and by taking the symmetric part before the Gram normalisation \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd} when building $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$.

\paragraph{Mechanics at $t^n$ (weak form).}
Find $\mathbf{u}_h^{n}\in V_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{v}_h\in V_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-mech-unified}
a_u(\mathbf{u}_h^{n},\mathbf{v}_h;\rho^{n},\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n}) \;=\; \ell_u(\mathbf{v}_h),
\end{equation}
with the bilinear and linear forms
\begin{align*}
a_u(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{v};\rho,\widehat{\mathbf{A}})
&:= \big(2\,\mu(\rho)\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{w}),\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})\big)
\,+\, \big(\lambda(\rho)\,\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{w}),\,\mathrm{tr}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})\big) \\
&\quad +\, \big(\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\,E(\rho)\,\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{w})\rangle,\,
\langle \widehat{\mathbf{A}},\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})\rangle\big), \\[2mm]
\ell_u(\mathbf{v}) &:= \langle \mathbf{t},\mathbf{v} \rangle_{\Gamma_N},
\end{align*}
where $\mu(\rho)$, $\lambda(\rho)$, and $E(\rho)$ are from \eqref{eq:E-rho-nd}, and $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n}$ is built from $\mathbf{A}^{n}$ via the Gram normalisation \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}.

\paragraph{Stimulus (Backward--Euler weak form).}
Given $\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:stim-psi-nd}, find $S_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $q_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-stim-unified}
\left(\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n} S_h^{n}, q_h\right) \;+\; \kappa_S\,(\nabla S_h^{n}, \nabla q_h) \;+\; \tau_S\,(S_h^{n}, q_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{c_S}{\Delta t^n} S_h^{n-1}, q_h\right) \;+\; \big(r_S(\psi(\mathbf{u}^{n},\rho^{n},\mathbf{A}^{n})-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}), q_h\big).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Density (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})$ from \eqref{eq:Drho-nd} and the split $|S^{n}|_{\varepsilon}$, $S^{n,\pm}_{\varepsilon}$ from \eqref{eq:smooth-splits-nd}, find $\rho_h^{n}\in Q_h$ such that for all $p_h\in Q_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-rho-unified}
\left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n}, p_h\right) \;+\; \big(\mathbf{D}_\rho(\widehat{\mathbf{A}}^{\,n})\,\nabla \rho_h^{n}, \nabla p_h\big)
\;+\; \big(|S^{n}|_{\varepsilon}\,\rho_h^{n}, p_h\big)
\;=\; \left(\frac{1}{\Delta t^n}\rho_h^{n-1}, p_h\right) \;+\; \big(S^{n,+}_{\varepsilon}\rho_{\max} + S^{n,-}_{\varepsilon}\rho_{\min}, p_h\big).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Direction/fabric (Backward--Euler weak form).}
With $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{u}^{n})=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}^{n})^\top\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}^{n})$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n})$ from \eqref{eq:M-hat-nd}, find $\mathbf{A}_h^{n}\in T_h$ such that for all $\mathbf{Q}_h\in T_h$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fe-A-unified}
\left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\mathbf{A}_h^{n}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; c_A\,\ell^2\,(\nabla \mathbf{A}_h^{n}, \nabla \mathbf{Q}_h)
\;+\; \tau_A\,(\mathbf{A}_h^{n}, \mathbf{Q}_h)
\;=\; \left(\frac{c_A}{\Delta t^n}\mathbf{A}_h^{n-1}, \mathbf{Q}_h\right) \;+\; \tau_A\,(\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u}^{n}), \mathbf{Q}_h).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remarks.}
(i) Dirichlet conditions for mechanics are imposed strongly in $V_h$; natural boundary conditions enter via $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\Gamma_N}$. (ii) The tensor unknown $\mathbf{A}_h^{n}$ is represented componentwise; symmetry is preserved at the continuous level and is respected numerically through the use of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\cdot)$ and the trace-safe normalisation \eqref{eq:Ahat-blend-nd}.

\section*{Fixed-point solver at a given time level: block Gauss--Seidel with Anderson acceleration}
\label{sec:fp-aa}
We describe the nonlinear solver used to obtain $(\mathbf{u}^n,S^n,\rho^n,\mathbf{A}^n)$ at a fixed time level $t^n$ from the Backward--Euler subproblems \eqref{eq:be-mech}--\eqref{eq:be-A}. Let
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{X} \;:=\; V\times Q\times Q\times T,\qquad
\mathbf{x} \;=\; (\mathbf{u},S,\rho,\mathbf{A}) \in \mathcal{X},
\end{equation*}
with the product inner product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product-norm}
\langle \mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle_{\mathcal{X}} \;=\;
\alpha_u\,(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}),\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})) \;+\;
\alpha_S\,(S,q) \;+\; \alpha_\rho\,(\rho,p) \;+\; \alpha_A\,(\mathbf{A},\mathbf{Q}) ,
\end{equation}
for $\mathbf{y}=(\mathbf{v},q,p,\mathbf{Q})$ and positive weights $\alpha_\bullet$. In the implementation, the \emph{merit/stopping} metric is a projected 2--norm on the flattened state with RMS-per-DOF block weights (i.e., $w_i=1/N_i$) that projects out Dirichlet DOFs in mechanics; the flattened state order for norms and mixing is $(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A},S)$.

\paragraph{Block map.}
Define the block Gauss--Seidel map $\mathcal{G}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}$ by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:block-map}
\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{u},S,\rho,\mathbf{A})
:=
\Big(\ \underbrace{\mathsf{U}(\rho,\mathbf{A})}_{\text{mech}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{S}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})}_{\text{stim}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{R}(S,\mathbf{A})}_{\text{density}},\ \underbrace{\mathsf{A}(\mathbf{u})}_{\text{fabric}}\ \Big),
\end{equation}
where each component is the unique solution of the corresponding linear elliptic BE subproblem at time $t^n$: mechanics uses coefficients from $(\rho,\widehat{\mathbf{A}})$, stimulus is driven by $\psi(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})$, density uses $|S|_\varepsilon$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{A}}$ frozen at the argument, and fabric uses the target $\widehat{\mathbf{M}}(\mathbf{u})$.

The fixed point at $t^n$ solves
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp-residual}
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}^n)\;:=\;\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^n)-\mathbf{x}^n \;=\; \mathbf{0}\quad\text{in }\mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block Gauss--Seidel iteration.}
Starting from $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}=(\mathbf{u}^{(0)},S^{(0)},\rho^{(0)},\mathbf{A}^{(0)})$, perform the sweep
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gs-sweep}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{u}^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{U}\big(\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{A}^{(m)}\big),\\
S^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{S}\big(\mathbf{u}^{(m+1)},\rho^{(m)},\mathbf{A}^{(m)}\big),\\
\rho^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{R}\big(S^{(m+1)},\mathbf{A}^{(m)}\big),\\
\mathbf{A}^{(m+1)} &:= \mathsf{A}\big(\mathbf{u}^{(m+1)}\big).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Let $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}:=\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}^{(m)})$ denote the result of \eqref{eq:gs-sweep}. No external scalar damping $\omega_m$ is used; instead, damping/backtracking and step limiting are handled inside the accelerator (see below). Dirichlet DOFs in mechanics are always taken from the fresh GS result $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$ when forming any mixed iterate.

\paragraph{Anderson acceleration (Type~I with $\sum\alpha=1$).}
We use a global, MPI-aware Type~I Anderson (Pulay) accelerator built on \emph{residual} snapshots
\(
r^{(j)}:=x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(j)}-\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}
\)
and corresponding states $\mathbf{x}^{(j-1)}$. With window length $p$ (capped by $m$), form the residual stack $R=[r^{(m-p+1)},\dots,r^{(m)}]^\top$ and the \emph{global} Gram matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-gram}
H \;:=\; R\,R^\top \quad(\text{reduced across MPI ranks}),
\end{equation}
then solve the equality--constrained, Tikhonov-regularized system for the weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in\mathbb{R}^p$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kkt}
\begin{bmatrix} H + \lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} I & \mathbf{1} \\ \mathbf{1}^\top & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\alpha} \\ \eta \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{0} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\qquad
\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; \lambda\,\max\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{tr}(H)}{p},\,1\right).
\end{equation}
The accelerated candidate is then assembled as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aa-update}
\mathbf{y} \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \alpha_j\Big(\mathbf{x}^{(m-p+j)} + \beta\,r^{(m-p+j+1)}\Big),\qquad
\mathbf{s}:=\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)},
\end{equation}
with a \emph{step limiter} enforcing $\|\mathbf{s}\|_2 \le \mathrm{factor}\,\|r^{(m+1)}\|_2$ (user parameter). The mixed iterate is $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}+\mathbf{s}$, after which mechanics Dirichlet DOFs are overwritten by the fresh GS values.

\paragraph{Projected-residual merit, safeguard, and backtracking.}
The acceptance test uses a \emph{projected, RMS-per-DOF block-weighted} 2--norm on the flattened state: let $\mathsf{P}_u$ zero mechanics Dirichlet DOFs and choose weights $w_i=1/N_i$ for each block. Denote by $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{proj}}$ the resulting norm. With
\(
r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)} := \|x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}-\mathbf{x}^{(m)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}
\)
and the \emph{proxy} for a candidate $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ given by
\(
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} := \|\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}-x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}},
\)
we accept the Anderson step iff
\begin{equation}\label{eq:safeguard}
r_{\mathrm{proxy}}^{(m+1)} \;\le\; (1-\gamma)\,r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}.
\end{equation}
Otherwise we backtrack along $\mathbf{s}$ by halving ($\le$ a user cap) until \eqref{eq:safeguard} holds; if backtracking fails, we \emph{fall back} to the raw Picard/GS iterate $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$. This safeguard is \emph{cheap}: it requires no extra GS sweep because the proxy only uses differences of already known states.

\paragraph{Automatic restarts and conditioning.}
The accelerator maintains the best Picard merit seen so far and schedules a history reset (on the \emph{next} call) if any of the following triggers fire: (i) a streak of $\ge k$ consecutive AA rejections; (ii) Picard merit stalls, i.e., $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}$ exceeds the best so far by a user factor; (iii) the estimated condition number of $H+\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}I$ exceeds a threshold. In case of linear-algebra failure in \eqref{eq:kkt}, uniform weights are used as a safe fallback. No preconditioner-driven resets are used; preconditioner maintenance (if any) is decoupled from Anderson.

\paragraph{Stopping and logging.}
Let $x_{\mathrm{next}}$ be the accepted iterate (AA, backtracked-AA, or Picard fallback). Define the projected-residual merit of the chosen step by
\(
r_{\mathrm{acc}}^{(m+1)} := \|x_{\mathrm{next}}-x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}\|_{\mathrm{proj}}
\)
(when $x_{\mathrm{next}}\equiv x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$ this equals $r_{\mathrm{Pic}}^{(m+1)}$). The loop stops once $r_{\mathrm{acc}}^{(m+1)}<\mathrm{tol}$ and at least a user-specified minimum number of subiterations has been taken. A separate \emph{blockwise relative iterate change} is recorded for diagnostics but is not used for stopping.

\paragraph{Well-posedness of subsolves.}
At each evaluation of $\mathcal{G}$ the four subproblems are uniformly elliptic (for fixed arguments) and admit unique solutions in the chosen finite-dimensional spaces: (i) mechanics has a coercive bilinear form by the density floor and $\nu\in(-1,\tfrac12)$, (ii) stimulus is SPD by $(c_S/\Delta t^n+\tau_S)$ with $\kappa_S\ge0$, (iii) density is SPD by $(1/\Delta t^n)+|S|_\varepsilon\ge 1/\Delta t^n$ together with the elliptic floor in $\mathbf{D}_\rho$, and (iv) fabric is SPD by $(c_A/\Delta t^n)+\tau_A>0$ and $c_A\ell^2>0$. Thus each application of $\mathcal{G}$ is well-defined.

\paragraph{Convergence discussion.}
On bounded sets where the smooth gates/clamps are Lipschitz, $\mathcal{G}$ is locally Lipschitz. If the induced modulus $L<1$, the undamped Gauss--Seidel iteration is linearly convergent. The safeguard \eqref{eq:safeguard} enforces a strict improvement over the contemporaneous Picard residual (by factor $1-\gamma$); combined with step limiting and automatic restarts, this yields robust global behavior of the AA-wrapped method even when the least-squares system \eqref{eq:kkt} is poorly conditioned.

\paragraph{Algorithm (per time level $t^n$).}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Initialize $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}$ (from $t^{n-1}$ or a warm start) and the AA history; set counters/timers to zero.
\item Perform one GS sweep to obtain $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$ and set $\mathbf{x}^{(1)}\!\leftarrow\!x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(1)}$.
\item For $m=1,2,\dots$ until the stopping test holds:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform one GS sweep to get $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$.
  \item If AA is enabled, form $H$ from residual histories, solve \eqref{eq:kkt}, assemble $\mathbf{y}$, apply step limiting, and build $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ with mechanics Dirichlet DOFs taken from $x_{\mathrm{raw}}^{(m+1)}$.
  \item Evaluate the projected-residual merit and apply the safeguard/backtracking \eqref{eq:safeguard}; fall back to Picard if needed.
  \item Accept the step, update histories (and schedule a restart if a trigger fires), and log diagnostics.
  \end{enumerate}
\item Output $\mathbf{x}^{n}:=\mathbf{x}^{(m_\star)}$ for the first index $m_\star$ meeting the stopping criterion.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Physical Parameter Analysis and Model Calibration}

We adopt a trabecular control volume of edge length \(L=5\,\mathrm{mm}\), a widely used compromise between statistical representativeness and computational tractability for \(\mu\)FE-based homogenization of human trabecular bone. Cubic samples around \(5\,\mathrm{mm}\) have long been used to extract apparent stiffness by the six canonical load cases (three normal, three shear); periodic (PMUBC) boundary conditions provide unbiased estimates, while kinematic (KUBC) bounds are useful for verification. \cite*{Hollister1994,vanRietbergen1995,Pahr2008}

\paragraph{Elastic law and Poisson ratio.}
At the tissue scale, measurements place the Young's modulus in the high one- to low two-digit GPa range. At the apparent scale, modulus increases with bone volume fraction and fabric; both classical tests and \(\mu\)FE-based regressions support power-law forms. We therefore use
\(
E(\rho)=E_{\mathrm{tiss}}\big(\rho/\rho_{\mathrm{tiss}}\big)^{n}
\)
with \(E_{\mathrm{tiss}}=18\,\mathrm{GPa}\) and \(n\in[1.7,2.2]\) as a defensible range; \(\nu=0.30\pm0.05\) is standard in hFE practice. \cite*{Helgason2008,Rho1993,CarterHayes1977}

\paragraph{Trabecular fabric and anisotropy control.}
Directionality is characterized by a unit-trace orientation/fabric tensor \(\widehat{\mathbf A}\). We introduce a scalar gain \(\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\) that multiplies a rank-one stiffness contribution aligned with \(\widehat{\mathbf A}\), thereby tuning the ratio \(E_{\max}/E_{\min}\). Mid-density human trabecular bone typically exhibits moderate elastic anisotropy \(E_{\max}/E_{\min}\approx 1.7\text{--}2.0\); we target this range during calibration. \cite*{Hollister1994,vanRietbergen1995}

\paragraph{Reference SED for mechanoregulation.}
We anchor remodeling to a physiologic reference centered on strain amplitudes of order \(10^{-3}\) (mechanostat). Using \(\psi=\tfrac12 E\,\varepsilon^2\), these strains correspond to reference strain--energy densities in the \(10\text{--}20\,\mathrm{kJ\,m^{-3}}\) range for trabecular tissue stiffness; we set \(\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}=15\,\mathrm{kJ\,m^{-3}}\). \cite*{Robling2006}

\paragraph{Stimulus transport and kinetics: roles of \(\boldsymbol{\kappa_S}\), \(\boldsymbol{\tau_S}\), and \(\boldsymbol{c_S}\).}
Osteocyte mechanotransduction combines rapid fluid-shear sensing and strain amplification at cell processes with solute transport through the lacunar--canalicular system (LCS). This motivates a diffusive--relaxing field \(S\) driven by local mechanical energy:
\((c_S/\Delta t+\tau_S)S-\kappa_S\nabla^2 S=(c_S/\Delta t)S^{\mathrm{old}}+r_S(\psi-\psi_{\mathrm{ref}})\).
Here, \(\kappa_S\) (\(\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-1}}\)) controls spatial spread, \(\tau_S\) (\(\mathrm{s^{-1}}\)) sets the intrinsic relaxation rate (\(t_c=1/\tau_S\)), and \(c_S\) (dimensionless) is a \emph{capacity} that lumps fast receptor/second-messenger kinetics, effectively low-pass filtering the stimulus at time step \(\Delta t\). We recommend \(c_S\in[10,40]\) (nominal \(20\)) to provide robust smoothing without overdamping; choose \(t_c\sim\) hours to separate fast stimulus equilibration from slow morphological updates. In the present implementation \(\kappa_S\) is \emph{isotropic}. \cite*{Wang2005PNAS,Price2011JBMR,Weinbaum1994,Robling2006}

\paragraph{From apparent modulus to load prescription.}
For uniaxial tests we prescribe the traction magnitude \(t_p = E_{\mathrm{app}}\,\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}\). In convergence studies we therefore: (i) compute \(E_{\mathrm{app}}\) by homogenization on the \(5\,\mathrm{mm}\) cube under PMUBC; (ii) select \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}=10^{-3}\) (and optionally \(1.5\times 10^{-3}\)); and (iii) set \(t_p\) accordingly, keeping boundary conditions identical across mesh refinements. \cite*{Hollister1994,vanRietbergen1995,Pahr2008}

\paragraph{Calibration protocol (single pass).}
(i) Fix \(L=5\,\mathrm{mm}\) and the sample's apparent density; (ii) set \(E_{\mathrm{tiss}}=18\,\mathrm{GPa}\), \(\nu=0.30\), and \(n=2.0\), then run the six canonical homogenization cases to obtain \(\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{app}}\) and principal \(E_i\); (iii) tune \(\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}\) to match \(E_{\max}/E_{\min}\) in the fabric axes; (iv) set \(\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}=15\,\mathrm{kJ\,m^{-3}}\); (v) choose \(t_c=1\,\mathrm{h}\), \(c_S=20\) (test \(10\text{--}40\)), and \(\kappa_S\) within LCS-consistent orders of magnitude. \cite*{Helgason2008,Pahr2008,Wang2005PNAS,Robling2006}

\paragraph{Baseline parameter set for spatial convergence (fixed across meshes).}
\emph{Geometry:} \(L=5\,\mathrm{mm}\). \
\emph{Elasticity:} \(E_{\mathrm{tiss}}=18\,\mathrm{GPa}\), \(n=2.0\), \(\nu=0.30\). \
\emph{Anisotropy:} \(\xi_{\mathrm{aniso}}=0.20\) (to be refined to match \(E_{\max}/E_{\min}\)). \
\emph{Mechanoregulation:} \(\psi_{\mathrm{ref}}=15\,\mathrm{kJ\,m^{-3}}\). \
\emph{Stimulus transport/kinetics:} \(t_c=1/\tau_S=1\,\mathrm{h}\), \(c_S=20\) (sensitivity \(10\text{--}40\)); \(\kappa_S\) isotropic and chosen within FRAP-based orders. \
\emph{Loading:} \(t_p=E_{\mathrm{app}}\times 10^{-3}\) (and \(1.5\times 10^{-3}\) for the higher level), using identical PMUBC across refinements.

\paragraph{KUBC homogenization under the adopted nondimensionalization.}
Let $\varepsilon_c:=u_c/L_c$, $\sigma_c:=E_0\,\varepsilon_c$, and $\psi_c:=E_0\,\varepsilon_c^2$ be the characteristic strain, stress, and strain–energy scales from the nondimensionalization in \S\ref{sec:strong-forms-nd}. All fields used in the homogenization below are \emph{dimensionless}; primes are dropped. For a prescribed macroscopic (symmetric) strain tensor $\mathbf{E}$ we impose the affine boundary field $\mathbf{u}_D(x)=\mathbf{E}x$ and solve the kinematic uniform boundary condition by symmetric Nitsche coupling on $\partial\Omega$ (with $\theta=1$): find $\mathbf{u}\in[H^1(\Omega)]^d$ such that for all $\mathbf{v}\in[H^1(\Omega)]^d$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kubc-nitsche-weak}
\begin{aligned}
(\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A}),\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v}))_{\Omega}
&\;-\;\langle \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n},\,\mathbf{v}\rangle_{\partial\Omega}
\;-\;\langle \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{v},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n},\,\mathbf{u}\rangle_{\partial\Omega}
\;+\;\Big\langle \frac{\gamma}{h}\,\mathbf{u},\,\mathbf{v}\Big\rangle_{\partial\Omega}
\\
&=\;-\;\langle \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{v},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathbf{n},\,\mathbf{u}_D\rangle_{\partial\Omega}
\;+\;\Big\langle \frac{\gamma}{h}\,\mathbf{u}_D,\,\mathbf{v}\Big\rangle_{\partial\Omega},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\cdot)$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\cdot,\rho,\mathbf{A})$ are given by \eqref{eq:mech-eps-nd}–\eqref{eq:stress-law-nd}, $h$ is a local facet size, and the penalty is chosen as $\gamma=\alpha\,E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}$ with $\alpha>0$ and a \emph{dimensionless} reference modulus $E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}$ (discussed below). The Hill–Mandel work equivalence holds in this setting:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hill-mandel-nd}
\overline{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}:\mathbf{E}
\;=\;\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\int_{\Omega}\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A}):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})\,\mathrm{d}x,
\qquad
\overline{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}:=\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\int_{\Omega}\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},\rho,\mathbf{A})\,\mathrm{d}x.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Six canonical tests and the apparent tensor.}
We drive six \emph{dimensionless} strain cases of engineering magnitude $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}>0$:
\[
\mathbf{E}^{(1)}=\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}\mathbf{e}_1\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_1,\quad
\mathbf{E}^{(2)}=\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}\mathbf{e}_2\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_2,\quad
\mathbf{E}^{(3)}=\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}\mathbf{e}_3\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_3,
\]
\[
\mathbf{E}^{(4)}=\tfrac12\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}(\mathbf{e}_2\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_3+\mathbf{e}_3\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_2),\quad
\mathbf{E}^{(5)}=\tfrac12\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}(\mathbf{e}_1\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_3+\mathbf{e}_3\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_1),\quad
\mathbf{E}^{(6)}=\tfrac12\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}(\mathbf{e}_1\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_2+\mathbf{e}_2\!\otimes\!\mathbf{e}_1),
\]
so that shear uses the engineering convention $\gamma_{ij}=2E_{ij}$. Let $s^{(k)}_{\mathrm{nd}}\in\mathbb{R}^6$ be the Voigt vector of $\overline{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}^{(k)}$ in the order $(11,22,33,23,13,12)$. The \emph{dimensionless} apparent stiffness is assembled columnwise as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kubc-C-nd}
\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{nd}}(:,k)\;=\;\frac{s^{(k)}_{\mathrm{nd}}}{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}},\qquad k=1,\dots,6,
\end{equation}
followed by minor–major symmetrization. Rotating $\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{nd}}$ to a fabric-aligned basis via $C'_{pqrs}=R_{pi}R_{qj}R_{rk}R_{sl}C_{ijkl}$ and inverting $\mathbf{S}'=(\mathbf{C}')^{-1}$ yields principal moduli $E_i^{\mathrm{nd}}$, shear $G_{ij}^{\mathrm{nd}}$, and Poisson ratios $\nu_{ij}$ (Voigt convention), all in the nondimensional setting.

\paragraph{Impact of nondimensionalization and unit recovery.}
By construction $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{\mathrm{dim}}=\sigma_c\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{\mathrm{nd}}$ and $\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{dim}}=\varepsilon_c\,\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{nd}}$. Consequently the dimensional and dimensionless apparent tensors are linked by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kubc-scale}
\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{dim}}
\;=\;\frac{\sigma_c}{\varepsilon_c}\,\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{nd}}
\;=\;E_0\,\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{nd}}.
\end{equation}
Thus \emph{all} reported elastic moduli scale linearly with $E_0$, while ratios such as $E_{\max}/E_{\min}$, $G_{ij}/E_k$, and $\nu_{ij}$ are \emph{invariant} to $(L_c,u_c)$ and to the choice of $E_0$. Average energy transforms as $\overline{\psi}^{\mathrm{dim}}=\psi_c\,\overline{\psi}^{\mathrm{nd}}$, consistent with \eqref{eq:hill-mandel-nd}. If a \emph{physical} macrostrain $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{dim}}$ is desired in KUBC, choose $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eng}}=\varepsilon_{\mathrm{app}}^{\mathrm{dim}}/\varepsilon_c$ when prescribing $\mathbf{u}_D$; conversely, dimensional results are obtained by multiplying $E_i^{\mathrm{nd}}$ and $G_{ij}^{\mathrm{nd}}$ by $E_0$, while $\nu_{ij}$ remains unchanged.

\paragraph{Penalty scaling in nondimensional form.}
In \eqref{eq:kubc-nitsche-weak} the penalty enters as $(\gamma/h)\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\partial\Omega}$ and must be comparable to a representative shear/Young modulus to guarantee coercivity. In dimensional variables one would set $\gamma^{\mathrm{dim}}=\alpha\,E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{dim}}$. Under our scaling, $h=h^{\mathrm{dim}}/L_c$ and $E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}=E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{dim}}/E_0$, so $(\gamma/h)$ remains dimensionless if $\gamma=\alpha\,E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}$. A robust choice is $E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}\approx \sup_{\Omega}E(\rho)$ (or a safe upper percentile) so that a mesh-independent $\alpha=\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ suffices; using $E_{\mathrm{ref}}^{\mathrm{nd}}\equiv 1$ is consistent provided $\alpha$ is chosen large enough to dominate the largest $E(\rho)$ realized by the current $(\rho,\mathbf{A})$ field. This keeps the KUBC bilinear form uniformly coercive in the nondimensional setting while preserving the expected dimensional scaling \eqref{eq:kubc-scale}.

\subsection*{Linear solver defaults and preconditioner maintenance}\label{subsec:ksp_defaults}
Unless stated otherwise, each linear block solve uses PETSc MINRES with GAMG preconditioning. Preconditioner maintenance is decoupled from the AA/GS logic and is triggered only when the previous Krylov solve exceeded a given iteration threshold; otherwise the preconditioner is reused.
